\begin{abstract}
\textbf{Introduction}
Biomedical time series analysis increasingly relies on complex models that sacrifice interpretability for performance, limiting clinical adoption. 
Current automated methods like MiniRocket produce thousands of opaque features, while interpretable
approaches use fixed feature sets that miss task-specific patterns. We introduce SublimeX, an open-source framework for
interpretable feature extraction from time series and spatial data that bridges this gap.
\textbf{Methods}
SublimeX performs supervised feature extraction: in each iteration, Bayesian optimization proposes a candidate segment-based feature, a model is trained on
the current feature set augmented with the candidate, and cross-validation performance guides feature selection. Each feature computes the mean over an
optimized segment of a signal transformation (raw, z-score, derivative, or FFT power). The search stops when additional features no longer improve model
performance, yielding compact, task-specific feature sets.
\textbf{Results}
Across seven biomedical benchmarks, SublimeX achieves performance comparable to state-of-the-art methods while using only 5--20
extracted features versus thousands for competing approaches: best RMSE on glucose forecasting (AZT1D, 31.11 vs.\ 31.18
MiniRocket), best AUC on gene expression prediction (REMC, $0.931 \pm 0.023$, tied with MiniRocket), 98.9\% accuracy on arrhythmia
classification (MITBIH), $0.818 \pm 0.024$ AUC on ICU mortality prediction (MIMIC-IV), and 86.0\% on activity recognition (PAMAP2).
\textbf{Discussion}
Discovered features align with established domain knowledge---
TSS-proximal histone enrichment for gene expression, recent
glucose trajectory and insulin/carbohydrate effects for glucose
forecasting, Glasgow Coma Scale dynamics and lactate levels for
ICU mortality---enabling clinicians to inspect and validate
model reasoning.
An ablation study confirms the importance of multi-transform search and iterative optimization. SublimeX is available as an open-source Python package via PyPI and GitHub.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Biomedical machine learning increasingly operates on time series and structured signals: continuous bedside monitoring (ECG, EEG, oxygenation, glucose),
longitudinal electronic health records, wearable inertial sensors, and spatially indexed molecular measurements such as epigenomic tracks.
These modalities are central to both clinical decision support and biomedical discovery \cite{morid2023time, patharkar2024predictive}.
However, in many biomedical settings the bottleneck is not only predictive accuracy, but also whether a model produces evidence that is actionable and
auditable by domain experts \cite{tonekaboni2019clinicians, rudin2019stop}.

Historically, three paradigms have dominated signal modeling. First, manual feature engineering uses domain knowledge to craft descriptors
with clear meaning (e.g., ECG morphology, EEG band power), but this approach is labor-intensive and may miss task-specific characteristics in heterogeneous cohorts \cite{fulcher2018feature, miotto2018deep}.
Second, automated feature extraction libraries compute large feature sets at scale (e.g., ROCKET \cite{dempster2020rocket}, MiniRocket \cite{dempster2021minirocket}, tsfresh \cite{christ2018tsfresh}), 
but they produce fixed feature sets that may include many irrelevant descriptors and can dilute interpretability \cite{henderson2021empirical}.
Third, deep learning learns representations end-to-end and often excels on benchmark tasks, yet the learned features are difficult to validate in
biomedical workflows where transparency is essential \cite{miotto2018deep, wang2022systematic}.

In parallel, shapelet-based methods use discriminative subsequences to build interpretable representations \cite{ye2009shapelets, hills2014shapelet,
grabocka2014learning}. However, shapelets are inherently limited to subsequence-shape matching and therefore cannot capture discriminative
information that is better characterized by regional statistics---such as mean amplitude, variance, or trend within a segment---or by frequency-domain
content \cite{middlehurst2024bakeoff}. This restricts their applicability to biomedical signals where periodic or spectral features are often the primary discriminators.

We present \textbf{SublimeX}, an interpretable interval-based feature extraction framework designed around three core principles that address the limitations of existing approaches:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Minimal feature set}: Rather than computing thousands of pre-defined features and
filtering post-hoc, SublimeX discovers task-specific features iteratively, adding one feature
at a time until no further improvement is observed. This yields compact representations tailored to each task.

\item \textbf{Interpretable features}: Each SublimeX feature has an explicit,
human-readable description: the mean computed over an optimized segment of a
specific signal transformation (raw, z-score, derivative, FFT power) on a
specific channel. Domain experts can directly inspect what information each feature captures.

\item \textbf{Flexible and extensible framework}: SublimeX's modular architecture allows users
to customize signal transformations, aggregation functions, and machine learning models,
enabling domain-specific adaptations while retaining the core optimization framework.
\end{enumerate}

We validate SublimeX through comprehensive evaluations on seven diverse real-world biomedical
tasks: arrhythmia classification on MIT-BIH \cite{MoodyMark1992,Goldberger2000}, emotion
classification from EEG (Emotions) \cite{bird2018study}, gene expression prediction from
histone modification profiles (REMC) \cite{Roadmap2015}, mortality prediction from MIMIC-IV
clinical time series \cite{Johnson2023MIMICIV}, glucose forecasting (AZT1D)
\cite{khamesian2025azt1d}, human activity recognition from IMU sensors (PAMAP2)
\cite{Reiss2012PAMAP2}, and voice pathology detection (SVD) \cite{svd_database}. Crucially,
the inclusion of spatial epigenomic data (REMC) illustrates that SublimeX is not limited to temporal data but generalizes to any sequential data. 
We compare our approach against established automated baselines under matched splits and metrics.
Furthermore, we provide an ablation study to assess the
importance of the key design choices of SublimeX, provide
post-hoc SHAP analyses on two contrasting domains---gene
expression prediction and glucose forecasting---to
interpret the discovered features.

\section{Related Work}

We review four paradigms for time series modeling---manual feature engineering, automated
feature extraction, shapelet-based methods, and deep learning---before situating SublimeX
within this landscape.

\subsection{Manual and automated feature engineering}
Traditional biomedical time series modeling relies on domain-driven descriptors (e.g., ECG
morphology, EEG band power) that offer clear meaning and support targeted error analysis
\cite{woldaregay2019data, fulcher2018feature}. However, manual engineering is labor-intensive
and may miss task-specific characteristics across heterogeneous cohorts \cite{miotto2018deep}.

Automated libraries address this by computing large feature sets at scale. tsfresh
\cite{christ2018tsfresh} and catch22 \cite{lubba2019catch22} provide statistical coverage with
varying degrees of interpretability, while hctsa and TSFEL \cite{fulcher2017hctsa,
barandas2020tsfel} offer even larger descriptor sets. ROCKET and MiniROCKET
\cite{dempster2020rocket, dempster2021minirocket} achieve state-of-the-art accuracy through
random convolutional kernels with minimal computational overhead. These approaches compute
fixed feature sets regardless of task requirements: high-dimensional methods like MiniRocket
(10,000 features) sacrifice interpretability, while compact methods like catch22 (22 features)
may miss task-specific patterns not covered by their predefined descriptors.

\subsection{Shapelet-based methods}
Shapelets---discriminative subsequences whose presence distinguishes classes---provide both
features and interpretable explanations \cite{ye2009shapelets, hills2014shapelet}. The
Shapelet Transform \cite{hills2014shapelet} converts time series into distance-based feature
vectors, while fast discovery schemes \cite{rakthanmanon2013fast_shapelets} and
optimization-based learning \cite{grabocka2014learning, hou2016efficient_shapelets} reduce
computational cost. Random Dilated Shapelet Transform (RDST) \cite{guillaume2022random}
introduces dilation to capture multi-scale patterns efficiently. While shapelets excel at
identifying discriminative shapes, they focus on subsequence matching rather than regional
statistics like amplitude or trend.

\subsection{Deep learning approaches}
Deep learning methods learn representations end-to-end and often achieve benchmark-leading performance on time series tasks \cite{miotto2018deep, morid2023time}. Convolutional neural
networks (CNNs) have shown strong results in arrhythmia detection \cite{acharya2017ecg_deep, hannun2019cardiologist}, while recurrent architectures (LSTMs) excel at activity recognition
\cite{ordonez2016deep_activity, hammerla2016deep_activity}. 
These models can automatically discover complex temporal patterns without explicit feature engineering. However, their learned representations are difficult to interpret, 
validate against domain knowledge, or audit for failure modes---critical limitations in clinical settings where transparency is essential \cite{rudin2019stop, tonekaboni2019clinicians, wang2022systematic}.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Overview}
SublimeX discovers interpretable features from time series and spatial data through a supervised, iterative feature discovery process. 
The core idea is simple: identify discriminative segments within different signal transformations and regions and compute the mean over those segments, which then become features for a machine learning model. 
An overview of the SublimeX methodology is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:sublime_methodology}.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.65\textwidth,keepaspectratio]{images/flowchart.png}
  \caption{SublimeX methodology overview: The process begins with time series or spatial data, then applies multiple signal transformations to create parallel views of the data.
  Iterative feature discovery then: (1) proposes candidate feature parameters (channel, transform, segment boundaries), (2) extracts the segment from the transformed signal, 
  (3) computes the mean over the segment, and (4) evaluates model performance via cross-validation.
  The best feature is appended to the feature set and the procedure repeats until adding a new feature no longer improves performance.}
  \label{fig:sublime_methodology}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Signal Transformation}
The first stage applies a set of signal transformations to all input channels, creating
multiple views of the data. The default transformation library includes four transforms:
\textbf{Raw} (identity transform preserving the original signal values), \textbf{Z-score}
(per-sample standardization $z_t = (x_t - \mu) / \sigma$, removing amplitude differences to
focus on shape), \textbf{Derivative} (first-order gradient $\nabla x_t$, capturing rate of
change and highlighting transitions), and \textbf{FFT Power} (power spectrum $|F(x)|^2$
interpolated back to the original time length, capturing frequency content). Users can extend
the library with domain-specific transformations, enabling incorporation of expert knowledge into the feature discovery process.

All transformations are precomputed once and stored, creating parallel views of the input signals. During feature optimization, the optimizer jointly selects which transformation and
channel to use for each feature, allowing discriminative information to emerge in whichever signal representation is most predictive for the task.

\subsection{Segment-Based Feature Optimization}
The core of SublimeX is a supervised optimization loop that discovers discriminative segment-based features. Each feature is defined by three
parameters: \textbf{Channel index} (which input channel to examine for multivariate data), \textbf{Transform type} (which signal transformation to
use), and \textbf{Segment boundaries} (start and end positions defining the temporal region).

For each candidate feature proposed by the optimizer (Optuna with a TPE sampler), SublimeX extracts the segment from the transformed signal and
computes the mean over the segment to obtain the feature value for each sample.
The candidate feature is appended to the existing feature matrix, a model is trained on the augmented feature set, and performance is evaluated on the validation set. 
This supervised evaluation ensures that every retained feature demonstrably improves predictive performance. 
After optimizing one feature, SublimeX keeps the feature and repeats the search for the next feature where during model training all prior features are also kept in the feature set. 
The loop stops when the best new candidate fails to improve validation performance. 

\subsection{Implementation Details}
The default configuration used in our experiments is as follows. The optimizer uses Optuna with a Tree-structured Parzen Estimator (TPE) sampler \cite{bergstra2011tpe}, running 300 trials per feature. 
Internal validation uses a single deterministic 50/50
train/validation split (fixed random seed) to evaluate candidate
features during optimization; every Optuna trial within a feature
search is therefore evaluated on exactly the same held-out samples,
ensuring that performance comparisons are not confounded by split
variability. 
The default machine learning model is LightGBM with maximum tree depth of 5.

A key strength of SublimeX is its modular, extensible design: users can customize virtually every component of the pipeline to suit domain-specific needs. 
Specifically, SublimeX accepts custom objective functions (e.g. one may not want to use the mean aggregation function but instead the median or the maximum), custom ML models (any
scikit-learn-compatible model with fit/predict interface; default: LightGBM; alternatives include linear models, SVMs, or neural networks), and custom transforms 
(domain-specific signal transformations beyond the default library). 
This flexibility enables practitioners to incorporate domain knowledge directly into the feature discovery process while retaining the core optimization loop. 
SublimeX is available as an official Python package on PyPI (see \url{https://pypi.org/project/sublimex/}). We ran our experiments on a MacBook Pro M2 using 12 cores and 32GB of RAM with Python 3.13.5.

\subsection{Baselines}
\label{sec:baselines}
We compare SublimeX against a comprehensive suite of time series analysis methods: tsfresh \cite{christ2018tsfresh}, catch22 \cite{lubba2019catch22}, MiniRocket \cite{dempster2021minirocket}, 
RDST \cite{guillaume2022random}, and a 1D CNN. For all feature-based methods (tsfresh, catch22, MiniRocket, RDST, SublimeX), we train a LightGBM
classifier \cite{ke2017lightgbm} on the extracted features using default hyperparameters. This ensures that performance differences reflect the quality of the extracted features rather than
classifier tuning. Detailed implementation specifications are provided in the Supplementary Material.

\subsection{Evaluation}
We evaluate all methods using stratified 5-fold cross-validation for most datasets. For PAMAP2, we follow Tello et al.\ \cite{tello2025contrasting} protocol with 512-sample windows
(5.12 seconds) and 100-sample shift (1 second), combined with leave-one-subject-out (LOSO) cross-validation to ensure subject-independent evaluation, as recommended by recent work on
HAR evaluation protocols \cite{tello2025contrasting, geissler2024power}. For the AZT1D glucose forecasting dataset, we use a one time temporal 80\%/20\% train/test split per patient to prevent data leakage.
For REMC, we run experiments on all 56 cell lines from the Roadmap Epigenomics Consortium, evaluating each cell line independently with 5-fold CV.
Results are aggregated as mean $\pm$ standard deviation across folds for standard datasets, across all cell lines and folds for REMC, across
all subjects for PAMAP2 (8 LOSO folds), and across all subjects for AZT1D. Standard datasetsuse the same 5-fold CV protocol as the main evaluation. Evaluation metrics are task-dependent:
AUC for REMC and MIMIC, binary accuracy for SVD, multi-class classification accuracy for Emotions, MITBIH, PAMAP2 and root mean squared error (RMSE) for AZT1D.
An overview of the evaluation metrics can be found in Table~\ref{tab:datasets}.

\subsection{Transform Composition and Pattern Stability}

Beyond predictive accuracy, a key advantage of SublimeX is that its learned features can be inspected.
We thus investigated which signal transforms and inside a given signal which segment were selected by SublimeX, and how stable these selections are across five cross-validation folds.
Note that our iterative feature discovery process is by no means deterministic as every fold different samples are used in the feature extraction process.
Figure~\ref{fig:performance_analysis} summarizes which signal transformations SublimeX selects and how stable the discovered patterns are across cross-validation folds.

Panel~A aggregates the signal transforms chosen across all extracted features per dataset, revealing which representations SublimeX favors in each domain.
For MITBIH arrhythmia classification, derivatives (49\%) and z-score normalization (32\%) capture QRS morphology independent of amplitude. For AZT1D glucose forecasting, 
raw values (44\%) and derivatives (31\%) dominate, reflecting the importance of both absolute glucose levels and rates of change for predicting future glucose dynamics. 
For amplitude-driven tasks like PAMAP2 (IMU sensors) and REMC (ChIP-seq signal), raw values dominate (58--64\%). MIMIC shows balanced usage across all four transforms, 
while Emotions and SVD combine raw values with FFT power.

Panel~B focuses on the \emph{first} feature only, showing its mean segment position across cross-validation folds with error bars indicating positional spread.
Here, we focus only on the first extracted feature because it usually carries the most predictive weight and is not dependent on any prior extracted features.

reflecting multiple valid representations of histone modification patterns near the TSS. Emotions and SVD show moderate positional variability (std~=~0.23 and~0.21) despite perfect transform consistency, 
suggesting multiple discriminative regions exist and the optimization finds different local optima.
MIMIC shows the lowest transform consistency (T:0.4), indicating that multiple signal representations carry complementary prognostic information.

subset and evaluated on all three splits, revealing how performance
evolves during feature discovery.

MITBIH and REMC show tight train--validation--test convergence
(validation--test gaps of ${\sim}0.1\%$ and ${\sim}0.5\%$,
respectively), indicating that discovered features capture
genuine discriminative patterns in datasets with localized,
consistent signal structure (QRS morphology, TSS-proximal
histone enrichment).
Emotions and MIMIC show large train--test gaps
(${\sim}6\%$ and ${\sim}17\%$) driven by LightGBM overfitting
to the small training set, but their validation--test gaps
remain small (${\sim}2\%$ and ${\sim}1.6\%$), indicating
that the feature search itself generalizes well.
SVD shows moderate validation--test separation
(${\sim}6\%$), suggesting that the feature search partially
overfits to the optimization split on this challenging task.
PAMAP2 exhibits the most extreme pattern: training accuracy
reaches 100\% and validation 98.7\%, yet test accuracy
plateaus at ${\sim}73\%$ (validation--test gap
${\sim}26\%$). This large gap arises because the internal
50/50 train/validation split shares the same subjects,
whereas LOSO evaluation tests on an entirely unseen subject,
confirming that the discovered features capture
subject-specific movement patterns rather than
generalizable activity signatures.

\subsection{Ablation Study}

We ablate key design choices across seven datasets.
All ablation experiments are evaluated on a single fold
only (fold~1), so no standard deviations are reported and
Baseline scores may differ slightly from the multi-fold
averages in Table~\ref{tab:results}.
To further reduce computational cost, REMC experiments
use only the first cell line (E003) and PAMAP2 experiments
use only the first subject (single LOSO fold).
Our default configuration uses mean aggregation, 300 trials
per feature, a single 50/50 train/validation split for
internal evaluation, the full transform library (raw,
z-score, derivative, FFT power), and the TPE sampler.
Table~\ref{tab:ablation} reports score, feature count,
and runtime (in seconds) for each variant.

We evaluate seven variants against the Baseline. Baseline serves as our default configuration using mean aggregation with LightGBM and the TPE sampler.
\emph{Optimized Aggregate} expands the search space to include eight aggregation functions (mean, min, max, range, std, median, argmin, argmax). 
\emph{Pattern Search} searches for discriminative quadratic B\'ezier patterns using Euclidean distance similarity, similar to shapelet-based methods such as RDST \cite{guillaume2022random}.
\emph{Decision Tree} replaces LightGBM with a Decision Tree (max\_depth=5) during optimization and test set prediction, testing whether a simpler internal model improves feature quality. 
\emph{1000 Trials} increases the budget from 300 to 1000 trials per feature.
\emph{Raw Only} disables signal transforms, using only raw signals. \emph{NSGA-II} replaces TPE with a multi-objective evolutionary algorithm. 
\emph{Parallel} extracts all features simultaneously in a single optimization run (using the same number of features as Baseline) rather than iteratively, 
testing whether joint optimization outperforms greedy sequential search.

The results reveal several insights. Signal transforms are
critical: the Raw Only variant shows degradation on Emotions
(93.2\% vs.\ 94.4\% Parallel) and MITBIH (98.3\% vs.\
98.9\% NSGA-II), confirming that multi-representation
search across raw, z-score, derivative, and FFT power
transforms is essential for capturing discriminative
information. Despite its simplicity, mean aggregation is
effective: the Baseline achieves best or near-best
performance on Emotions, REMC, MITBIH, and SVD,
demonstrating that regional mean statistics capture
discriminative information effectively.
On AZT1D, NSGA-II achieves the best RMSE (30.81),
closely followed by the Aggregate variant (30.88),
while Raw Only scores worst (31.25), underscoring
the value of multi-transform search for glucose
forecasting.
The Decision Tree variant, which uses a depth-5 decision
tree for both optimization and final prediction, shows
substantial performance degradation compared to
LightGBM-based variants (e.g., 96.2\% vs.\ 98.9\% on
MITBIH, 0.771 vs.\ 0.847 Baseline on MIMIC-IV),
confirming that model capacity matters for test-time
evaluation even when feature discovery is identical.

On MIMIC-IV, the Raw Only variant achieves the highest single-fold AUC (0.859), outperforming all other variants including the Baseline (0.847 on fold 1),
indicating that for clinical time series with strong raw-signal discriminability, removing frequency-domain transforms can reduce noise in the feature search. 
Additional trials show diminishing returns: the 1000 Trials variant does not consistently outperform the Baseline (300 trials), indicating
that the default budget is sufficient for most tasks. NSGA-II performs
comparably to TPE across datasets, with tied best performance on MITBIH
(0.989). The Parallel variant achieves strong performance on Emotions (94.4\%
vs.\ 94.8\% Baseline on fold 1), demonstrating that joint optimization of all
features can approach greedy sequential search performance. However, Parallel
shows mixed results across datasets: it matches Baseline feature counts exactly
(by design) but achieves lower performance on MITBIH (98.6\% vs.\ 98.9\% on
fold 1), REMC (0.926 vs.\ 0.928 on fold 1), and SVD (66.8\% vs.\ 69.3\% on
fold 1), suggesting that the iterative greedy strategy captures complementary
information that joint optimization misses. Notably, Parallel is substantially
faster (5--77 seconds vs.\ hundreds or thousands for iterative variants),
making it attractive for rapid prototyping despite the performance trade-off.

\subsection{Domain-Specific Feature Interpretation}

To demonstrate SublimeX's interpretability across different
biomedical domains, we apply SHAP \cite{lundberg2017unified}
analysis to two tasks with distinct clinical contexts: REMC
gene expression prediction and AZT1D glucose forecasting
(Figure~\ref{fig:domain_interpretation}).

\subsubsection{REMC: Gene Expression Prediction from
Histone Modifications}

In molecular biology, histone modifications are chemical
tags attached to histone proteins---the ``spools'' around
which DNA is wound. These tags act as molecular switches
that control whether nearby genes are turned on (expressed)
or off (silenced), without altering the DNA sequence itself.
Five common modifications---H3K4me3, H3K4me1, H3K36me3,
H3K9me3, and H3K27me3---form a well-characterized
regulatory code: certain marks (e.g.\ H3K4me3) are
consistently enriched near the start of active genes,
while others (e.g.\ H3K27me3) accumulate at silenced loci
\cite{karlic2010histone, howe2017h3k4me3}.

Using the 12~SublimeX features extracted for cell line E003
(fold~1), we train LightGBM on the extracted features alone
(AUC~=~0.928) and compute SHAP values.
Panels~A and~B focus on the most important feature,
H3K4me3 raw 65--96---the mean H3K4me3 enrichment signal
in the region immediately surrounding the transcription
start site (TSS), where genes begin to be read. Panel~A
shows that actively expressed genes exhibit a strong
enrichment peak in this region, while silenced genes remain
flat. Panel~B confirms clear class separation (mean 16.9
for high expression vs.\ 4.0 for low expression).

Panel~C shows the SHAP beeswarm plot for all 12~features.
The most important feature is H3K4me3 raw 65--96
(mean absolute SHAP~=~1.47), followed by H3K36me3 raw
0--61 (mean absolute SHAP~=~0.86), which captures
H3K36me3 enrichment along the gene body---a hallmark of
actively transcribed genes. The repressive mark H3K27me3
ranks third (mean absolute SHAP~=~0.51), consistent
with its known role in gene silencing. This ranking
precisely mirrors the established epigenomic hierarchy
\cite{karlic2010histone, howe2017h3k4me3}: the activating
mark near the TSS is the strongest predictor, followed by
the elongation-associated mark along the gene body, and
then the silencing mark.

\subsubsection{AZT1D: Glucose Forecasting from
Continuous Monitoring Data}

Continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) devices measure blood
sugar levels every five minutes, producing a dense time
series that captures the dynamic interplay between meals,
insulin doses, physical activity, and metabolic state.
Predicting future glucose levels (here, 60~minutes ahead)
is clinically important for people with diabetes, as it
enables proactive adjustments to insulin dosing and
carbohydrate intake to avoid dangerous hypo- or
hyperglycemic episodes.

Using the 16~SublimeX features extracted for AZT1D, we train LightGBM on the training set with the extracted features and compute SHAP values. Panels~D and~E
focus on the most important feature, CGM raw 16--23---the mean glucose level over the most recent 35 minutes of CGM history.
We group samples into ``rising'' vs.\ ``falling'' glucose by splitting at the
median of the 60-minute-ahead glucose change (the regression target), so that
we can compare class-conditional distributions despite the task being regression.
Panel~D shows that samples where glucose is about to rise (red) already have elevated recent glucose levels
(mean ${\sim}$164~mg/dL), while samples where glucose
is about to fall (blue) show higher recent readings
(mean ${\sim}$130~mg/dL) that are declining---a pattern
consistent with the post-meal glucose trajectory, where an
initial rise is followed by insulin-mediated decline.

Panel~F shows the SHAP beeswarm plot for all 16~features.
The dominant feature is CGM raw 16--23 (mean
absolute SHAP~=~11.77), confirming that the current
glucose trajectory is the primary predictor. The second
and third features---CGM deriv.\ 21--23 (mean
absolute SHAP~=~3.57) and CGM raw 22--23 (mean
absolute SHAP~=~2.94)---capture the instantaneous rate of
glucose change and the most recent glucose reading,
respectively. Notably, the fourth- and fifth-ranked features
are Insulin FFT 0--10 (mean absolute SHAP~=~1.60) and Carbs
deriv.\ 10--23 (mean absolute SHAP~=~1.41), reflecting the
frequency pattern of insulin dosing and the rate of
carbohydrate absorption effect, both of which directly
influence future glucose dynamics. This feature hierarchy
aligns with clinical intuition: current glucose level and
trend are the strongest short-term predictors, while insulin
and carbohydrate effects provide secondary but clinically
essential context for anticipating glucose excursions
\cite{khamesian2025azt1d}.

The fact that SublimeX autonomously discovers clinically
and biologically meaningful feature hierarchies in both
domains---the epigenomic activation hierarchy for gene

for diabetes management---without any domain-specific
guidance underscores its ability to identify interpretable,
actionable features from data alone.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Summary of Main Findings}

Across seven biomedical datasets, SublimeX achieves competitive
performance while using only 5--20 extracted features versus
thousands for competing approaches. It leads on AZT1D glucose
forecasting (31.11 RMSE) and ties with MiniRocket on REMC gene
expression ($0.931 \pm 0.023$ AUC across 56 cell lines), with
strong results elsewhere: $0.818 \pm 0.024$ AUC on MIMIC-IV,
98.9\% on MITBIH, 94.2\% on Emotions, and 86.0\% on PAMAP2.

Inspection of the discovered features reveals task-dependent
transform selection---derivatives and z-score for MITBIH (QRS
morphology), raw values for amplitude-driven tasks (PAMAP2, REMC),
and balanced usage for MIMIC---with high positional stability on
MITBIH and PAMAP2 and greater variability on REMC and MIMIC, where
multiple discriminative regions exist. Incremental feature analysis
(Section~\ref{sec:feature_analysis}) shows that SublimeX excels
when signal structure is localized and consistent: MITBIH and REMC
exhibit tight train--validation--test convergence (gaps
${\sim}0.1\%$ and ${\sim}0.5\%$), whereas PAMAP2 suffers a
validation--test gap of ${\sim}26\%$ because LOSO evaluation tests
on entirely unseen subjects whose movement patterns differ from
those seen during optimization.

Ablation experiments confirm that multi-transform search is
essential (Raw Only degrades Emotions and MITBIH), mean aggregation
is sufficient for most tasks, and the 300-trial default budget
shows diminishing returns when increased to 1000. The iterative
greedy search outperforms joint parallel optimization on several
datasets, indicating that sequential feature addition captures
complementary information. SHAP analysis on two contrasting
biomedical domains demonstrates that SublimeX autonomously
discovers meaningful feature hierarchies in both cases: the
established epigenomic hierarchy for REMC---H3K4me3 near the
TSS, H3K36me3 in the gene body, and the repressive mark H3K27me3
\cite{karlic2010histone, howe2017h3k4me3}---and the clinically
intuitive glucose-trend-then-treatment hierarchy for AZT1D, where
current glucose level and its rate of change dominate, followed by
insulin and carbohydrate effects
\cite{khamesian2025azt1d}. These results illustrate the
interpretability that compact, explicitly described features
afford across fundamentally different data modalities and clinical
questions.

\subsection{Comparison to Related Work}

SublimeX performance compares favorably with task-specific approaches reported in the literature. For MITBIH arrhythmia classification, deep learning
approaches typically achieve 94--99\% accuracy but require thousands of parameters and GPU inference \cite{acharya2017ecg_deep, hannun2019cardiologist}.
Our 98.9\% accuracy with interpretable features represents a practical configuration for clinical settings where model transparency and computational
simplicity are prioritized. For MIMIC mortality prediction, published AUCs range from 0.75 to 0.85 depending on the feature set and time horizon
\cite{harutyunyan2019multitask, purushotham2018benchmarking}. SublimeX's $0.818 \pm 0.024$ AUC (mean across 5 folds; fold 1: 0.847) falls within this
range while offering explicit, interpretable features that support clinical validation.

On PAMAP2 activity recognition with LOSO evaluation, SublimeX achieves
$0.860 \pm 0.081$ accuracy, while tsfresh achieves $0.898 \pm 0.076$. This
result must be interpreted in light of recent methodological critiques: Tello
et al.\ \cite{tello2025contrasting} demonstrated that conventional HAR
evaluation practices---combining sliding windows with standard k-fold
cross-validation---systematically overestimate accuracy by allowing temporal
leakage between training and test sets. Many published PAMAP2 results,
including the original 77.8\% accuracy \cite{reiss2013confidence} and deep
learning approaches reporting 85--90\% \cite{ordonez2016deep_activity}, used
evaluation protocols that may inflate performance estimates. Our LOSO protocol
follows Tello et al.'s recommendations, ensuring subject-independent evaluation
without temporal leakage. Notably, all methods show substantial inter-subject
variability on this task: CNN achieves $0.790 \pm 0.180$, and RDST achieves
$0.819 \pm 0.187$, indicating that subject-dependent generalization remains challenging regardless of method.

Biomedical deployment places constraints on learning systems beyond benchmark accuracy: clinicians and scientists must be able to \emph{inspect} what evidence
a model uses, relate it to domain knowledge, and understand when and why failures occur \cite{tonekaboni2019clinicians, caruana2015intelligible}.
SublimeX represents each sample through segment-based features with explicit, human-readable
descriptions, which is attractive for biomedical use because features are directly interpretable, localized to specific signal regions, and compact enough for
standard feature attribution methods (e.g., SHAP) to yield meaningful explanations \cite{fulcher2018feature}.

\subsection{Limitations}

SublimeX characteristics such as the iterative addition of features and the use of a predefined transform library can also be seen as a limitation. Runtime can
be substantial when increasing the optimization budget (e.g., 1000 trials per feature), and the best configuration settings (e.g.\ transform types, ML model)
can vary across datasets. The greedy iterative search may also miss globally optimal feature combinations that would require joint optimization. Our
incremental feature analysis (Section~\ref{sec:feature_analysis})
reveals that the feature search can overfit to the optimization
split, particularly for cross-subject tasks like PAMAP2 where the
internal validation set shares subjects with training but the test
set contains an entirely unseen subject
(validation--test gap ${\sim}26\%$).

Our baseline comparison does not include several state-of-the-art time series classification methods such as HIVE-COTEv2
\cite{middlehurst2021hivecote2}, InceptionTime \cite{fawaz2020inceptiontime}, or LSTM-based architectures \cite{ordonez2016deep_activity}, which have demonstrated strong performance on
standard benchmarks \cite{middlehurst2024bakeoff}. Additionally, we do not
evaluate on the UCR time series classification archive \cite{dau2019ucr}, which is the most widely used benchmark in the field. These choices are deliberate: 
our focus is on creating a flexible, customizable framework useful for diverse real-world biomedical datasets with clinically meaningful endpoints and large sample sizes, 
where interpretability and domain alignment matter as much as raw accuracy.
Many UCR datasets are small and lack the clinical context needed to assess whether discovered features are meaningful to domain experts.

\subsection{Future Directions}

Future work includes learned or domain-specific signal transformations that could capture task-specific patterns beyond the default library, more efficient
search strategies (e.g., early stopping, warm-starting from related tasks) to reduce computational cost, and additional search for signal transformation functions 
or segment summary statistics for better discriminative power.

More broadly, the bottom-up design philosophy underlying SublimeX---starting with a minimal model and iteratively adding complexity guided by task
performance---is not limited to segment-based feature extraction. The same principle could be applied to other model families: for instance, one could
begin with a very simple neural network and iteratively add layers, neurons, or architectural components until no further improvement is observed, yielding
task-adapted architectures that are as compact as possible. Exploring such extensions would generalize the supervised bottom-up paradigm beyond feature engineering to model construction itself.
The framework is actively maintained and under continued development by the authors; we welcome contributions from the community to
extend its capabilities to new domains and modalities. 

\section{Conclusion}
We introduced SublimeX, an interpretable feature extraction framework built around three core principles: minimal feature sets discovered
through iterative optimization, interpretable features with explicit descriptions, and a flexible framework enabling domain-specific customization.

Through extensive experiments across seven diverse biomedical datasets, we demonstrated that SublimeX achieves performance comparable to or exceeding
state-of-the-art methods while providing fully transparent, human-readable
features with dramatically reduced dimensionality. SublimeX achieves the best
performance on AZT1D glucose forecasting (31.11 RMSE) and ties with MiniRocket
for the best AUC on REMC gene expression prediction ($0.931 \pm 0.023$ across
56 cell lines), with competitive performance on MIMIC-IV, MITBIH and Emotions. 
For complex multi-class tasks like PAMAP2 activity recognition or SVD voice pathology detection, SublimeX still struggles. 
Domain-specific SHAP analysis on two contrasting tasks---gene
expression prediction from histone modifications and glucose
forecasting from continuous monitoring data---reveals that
discovered features align with established clinical and
biological knowledge in both domains, demonstrating autonomous
discovery of physiologically meaningful characteristics.

SublimeX addresses a critical need in biomedical machine learning: methods that
are not only accurate but also transparent enough for clinical validation and scientific insight.

\section{Supplementary Material}

\subsection{Baseline Implementations}

We compare SublimeX against a comprehensive suite of time series analysis methods, ranging from feature engineering to deep learning methods.
For feature-based methods (SublimeX, tsfresh, catch22, MiniRocket, RDST), we train a LightGBM classifier on the extracted features. CNN is trained end-to-end.

\subsubsection{tsfresh}
\textbf{tsfresh} \cite{christ2018tsfresh} extracts a comprehensive library of statistical and spectral characteristics from time series data.
We use \texttt{MinimalFCParameters} to balance feature coverage and computational cost, yielding 8 features per univariate series.
For multivariate time series, we extract features independently for each channel and concatenate them into a single feature vector.

\subsubsection{catch22}
\textbf{catch22} \cite{lubba2019catch22} provides a canonical set of 22 computationally efficient time series features, selected from the larger hctsa
library for their diversity and discriminative power.
For multivariate data, we compute all 22 features per channel and concatenate them, resulting in $22 \times n\_channels$ features.

\subsubsection{MiniRocket}
\textbf{MiniRocket} \cite{dempster2021minirocket} transforms time series via approximately 10,000 random convolutional kernels with fixed (non-learned) weights.
For each kernel, it extracts the proportion of positive values (PPV) after convolution, yielding a high-dimensional feature representation.
We use \texttt{MiniRocketMultivariate} from the sktime library, which natively handles multivariate inputs by applying kernels across all channels.
This method represents the ``accuracy-at-all-costs'' end of the spectrum, offering high performance but limited interpretability.

\subsubsection{RDST}
\textbf{RDST} \cite{guillaume2022random} (Random Dilated Shapelet Transform) is a fast and accurate shapelet-based approach that introduces dilation to capture 
shapelets at multiple scales with improved computational efficiency.
We configure RDST with \texttt{max\_shapelets=1000}. The transform generates distance-based features measuring how similar each time series is to the discovered shapelets.

\subsubsection{CNN}
\textbf{CNN}: A 1D Convolutional Neural Network trained end-to-end, representing the deep learning baseline.
We implement a 1D CNN using PyTorch with three convolutional layers (filters: 64, 128, 256) each containing convolution, batch normalization, ReLU
activation, max pooling, and dropout (0.2, 0.2, 0.3). The architecture uses kernel sizes of 7, 5, and 3 for the three layers
respectively, with appropriate padding to maintain temporal resolution. Adaptive average pooling aggregates features across the temporal dimension,
followed by fully connected layers (256$\rightarrow$128$\rightarrow$64$\rightarrow$output) with ReLU activations, batch normalization, and dropout (0.4, 0.3) for regularization.

We train with Adam optimizer (lr=$10^{-3}$, weight decay=$10^{-4}$), early stopping (patience=10), and learning rate reduction on plateau (factor=0.5, patience=3).
Input data is z-score normalized using training set statistics. The model automatically handles multivariate inputs by adjusting the number of input channels 
based on the dataset structure. Training uses 300 epochs with batch size 128 and 80/20 train/validation split for early stopping.

\subsection{Dataset Processing Details}
\label{sec:dataset_processing}

\subsubsection{AZT1D Preprocessing}

The AZT1D dataset is available via Zenodo (\url{https://zenodo.org/records/15094234}).
For this dataset, we implemented knowledge-driven response curve modeling based on established pharmacokinetic and glycemic response literature.
For insulin, we use a gamma-like function with 15-minute onset, 60-minute peak,
and 4-hour duration; for carbohydrates, we use 10-minute onset, 45-minute peak, and 3-hour duration.
These response curves model the temporal dynamics of physiological effects,
with insulin effects peaking around 60 minutes and persisting for up to 4
hours, while carbohydrate effects peak around 45 minutes and persist for up to
3 hours.

At each timepoint, we compute cumulative active effects by convolving all historical insulin doses and carbohydrate intakes with their respective
response curves. This creates interpretable time series that capture overlapping physiological
effects, enabling the model to understand how multiple insulin doses and meal intakes interact over time.

The preprocessing pipeline extracts 24 timepoints (2 hours of history at
5-minute intervals) for three physiological signals: CGM values, active insulin effects, and active carbohydrate effects.
The regression target is future glucose change $g_{t+h} - g_t$ where $h = 60$ minutes (12 timepoints ahead).
We also include the initial CGM value (CGM\_0) as a baseline feature to provide the current glucose context.

\subsubsection{MITBIH Preprocessing}

The MIT-BIH Arrhythmia Database is available via PhysioNet (\url{https://physionet.org/content/mitdb/1.0.0/}).
The database contains annotated ECG recordings from 48 patients.
We extract individual heartbeats using a fixed window size around annotated R-peak positions (100 samples before and 100 samples after, totaling 200 samples per beat).
When the window extends beyond signal boundaries, we pad with zeros to maintain consistent segment length.
Each beat is classified into one of five arrhythmia types: Normal (N), Left bundle branch block (L), Right bundle branch block (R), Atrial premature (A), and Premature ventricular contraction (V).

\subsubsection{Emotions Preprocessing}

The Emotions EEG dataset is available via Kaggle (\url{https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/birdy654/eeg-brainwave-dataset-feeling-emotions}).
The dataset contains brainwave recordings collected while participants experienced different emotional states (positive, neutral, negative).
We use raw time-domain EEG signals from 5 channels (AF3, F7, F3, FC5, T7) with 254 time points per channel, without any frequency-domain transformations or filtering.

\subsubsection{REMC Preprocessing}

The Roadmap Epigenomics Consortium data is available at \url{http://www.roadmapepigenomics.org/}.
Following our prior work on PatternChrome \cite{paul2025prediction}, we predict binary gene
expression (high/low) from histone modification ChIP-seq profiles from the Roadmap Epigenomics Consortium (all 56 cell lines, 19,795 genes).
We convert RefSeq to GENCODE identifiers, retaining 18,421 genes.
For each gene, we extract five histone modification signals (H3K4me1, H3K4me3, H3K36me3, H3K9me3, H3K27me3) from 5000 bp upstream and downstream of the TSS,
divided into 100 bp bins (100 bins per mark). Gene expression is binarized at the median level.

\subsubsection{MIMIC-IV Preprocessing}

The MIMIC-IV database is available via PhysioNet
(\url{https://physionet.org/content/mimiciv/}; requires credentialing)
\cite{Johnson2023MIMICIV}.
We predict mortality from ICU patients on mechanical ventilation using this database.
The observation window comprises the first 24 hours of mechanical ventilation.
Mortality is defined as death within 6 days after the observation window.

The dataset includes static features, aggregated clinical measurements, and multivariate time
series. Static features (21 total) include demographics (age, gender), comorbidities (diabetes,
hypertension, immunosuppression, renal failure, obesity, heart failure, liver disease, cancer),
BMI, and admission service categories. We exclude diagnostic flags (ARDS, Sepsis-3, SIRS,
anemia) from initial features to prevent data leakage, as these flags are derived from clinical
measurements already captured by the time series and aggregated features.
Aggregated features (13 total) are clinical measurements with low temporal
variation, averaged over the 24-hour window: laboratory values (creatinine,
platelets, WBC, INR, lactate, bilirubin), blood gases (PaCO2, pH), GCS verbal
and motor components, and vasopressor doses (norepinephrine, epinephrine, dobutamine).
Time series features (12 channels $\times$ 24 hours) capture measurements with
high temporal variation: vital signs (heart rate, respiratory rate, SpO2,
blood pressure, temperature), ventilator settings (FiO2, PEEP), PaO2, GCS eye opening, and urine output.
Subjects with fewer than 24 hours of data are excluded. Missing values in time series are imputed using forward-fill followed by
backward-fill along the temporal axis, with remaining gaps filled with zero.


\subsubsection{PAMAP2 Preprocessing}

The PAMAP2 dataset is available via the UCI ML Repository
(\url{https://archive.ics.uci.edu/dataset/231/pamap2+physical+activity+monitoring}).
The dataset contains recordings from 9 subjects performing 18 physical
activities while wearing 3 IMUs (wrist, chest, ankle) and a heart rate monitor.
Subject 109 is excluded from training and evaluation as they only performed one activity
(rope jumping), making LOSO evaluation invalid for that fold (8 subjects used).
Following the preprocessing protocol from Tello et al. \cite{tello2025contrasting}, we
use 27 sensor channels: 3D acceleration ($\pm$16g scale), 3D gyroscope, and
3D magnetometer from each of the three IMUs (9 channels $\times$ 3 IMUs).
We exclude temperature, heart rate, 3D acceleration ($\pm$6g scale), and 4D orientation channels.
We create 512-sample windows (5.12 seconds at 100Hz) with a 100-sample shift
(1 second), filtering transient activities (ID 0) to obtain 12 activity
classes: lying, sitting, standing, walking, running, cycling, Nordic walking,
watching TV, computer work, ascending/descending stairs, and vacuum cleaning.
Missing values are interpolated linearly and remaining NaNs filled with column means.

\subsubsection{SVD Preprocessing}

The Saarbr\"ucken Voice Database (SVD) \cite{svd_database} is available at \url{https://stimmdb.coli.uni-saarland.de}.
It is a publicly available collection of voice recordings from over 2{,}000
subjects, including healthy controls and patients with 71 different voice pathologies \cite{martinez2012voice}.
We use recordings of the phrase ``Guten Morgen, wie geht es Ihnen?'' (Good morning, how are you?).
All pathology types are included without filtering, representing a realistic hospital triage scenario.
Audio is truncated to 3 seconds maximum and resampled to 1{,}000 samples.
The final dataset contains 1{,}988 recordings: 634 healthy controls (31.9\%) and 1{,}354 pathological cases (68.1\%).
