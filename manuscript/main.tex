\begin{abstract}
We introduce \textbf{patX}, an open--source Python package that automatically
discovers compact, highly predictive, and interpretable patterns from biomedical
time--series and spatial data.
By framing pattern discovery as a constrained optimization problem over
B--spline templates, patX learns human--readable patterns that serve as
transparent input features for conventional machine--learning models.
We demonstrate patX on five diverse biomedical tasks: (i) glucose forecasting
(AZT1D), (ii) arrhythmia classification (MITBIH), (iii) seizure classification
(Bonn EEG), (iv) gene expression prediction from epigenetic signatures (REMC),
and (v) clinical outcome prediction (MIMIC--IV).
Across these tasks, patX outperforms or matches tsfresh, catch22, and
convolutional neural networks, while providing direct interpretability and
significant speed--ups at inference time.
We also demonstrate the interpretability of patX patterns by visualizing
patterns and the areas at which they are active.
Our tool is available at \url{https://github.com/Prgrmmrjns/patX} and offers a
simple interface for extracting patterns from time series and spatial data.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Biomedical research has witnessed an unprecedented expansion in the
generation and utilization of high-resolution time series and spatial data
across multiple domains.
From continuous physiological monitoring through electrocardiography (ECG)
and electroencephalography (EEG) to advanced spatially-resolved omics
technologies, these data modalities have become fundamental to modern
healthcare and biomedical discovery \cite{morid2023time, rood2022impact}.

Time series data in healthcare captures the temporal evolution of patient
health, offering crucial insights into disease progression, treatment
effectiveness, and physiological dynamics that static measurements cannot
provide \cite{patharkar2024predictive}.
Continuous monitoring systems such as smart watches or electroencephalography
devices generate vast streams of physiological signals---from heart rate
variability and blood pressure fluctuations to brain activity patterns---that
contain rich information about both normal physiology and pathological states
\cite{chaddad2023electroencephalography}.

The clinical applications of these data types are extensive and growing.
Electrocardiography (ECG) analysis supports arrhythmia detection and
cardiovascular risk stratification, with modern systems processing thousands
of heartbeats to identify subtle patterns indicative of cardiac pathology
\cite{anbalagan2023analysis}.
EEG monitoring enables real-time seizure detection, sleep stage
classification, and assessment of neurological function across various
conditions \cite{chaddad2023electroencephalography}.
In genomics and epigenomics, spatial profiling of chromatin modifications and
gene expression patterns provides insights into regulatory mechanisms
underlying development and disease \cite{yadav2022systems}.

\subsection{Existing Approaches to Time Series Analysis}
The analysis of biomedical time series and spatial data has evolved through
three different paradigms that represent fundamentally different philosophies
toward feature representation and model development.

Traditional approaches to biomedical signal analysis rely heavily on domain
expertise to manually craft features that capture relevant temporal and
spatial characteristics.
This paradigm involves extracting carefully designed statistical measures,
temporal properties, spectral features, and morphological characteristics
based on established understanding of physiological processes
\cite{fulcher2018feature}.
For ECG analysis, clinicians and engineers have developed features capturing
heart rate variability, QRS complex morphology, and ST-segment deviations
\cite{gelen2025novel, ansari2023deep}.
Similarly, EEG analysis traditionally focuses on frequency band power,
coherence measures, and event-related potentials derived from decades of
neurophysiology research \cite{moutonnet2025augmentation}.

This approach benefits significantly from the incorporation of clinical domain
knowledge and provides features with clear physiological interpretations that
align with medical understanding.
Manual feature engineering allows for precise targeting of known pathological
markers and enables clinicians to understand exactly which signal
characteristics drive model predictions \cite{gelen2025novel}.

However, manual feature engineering suffers from several critical limitations
that constrain its scalability and generalizability.
The feature engineering process is labor-intensive, requiring expertise in
both signal processing techniques and the specific clinical domain as well as
requiring a preceding detailed exploratory data analysis \cite{roe2020feature}.
This expertise requirement creates bottlenecks in model development and limits
the ability to rapidly adapt to new clinical problems or signal types.
Moreover, manually crafted features may miss subtle patterns that could be
crucial for accurate classification or prediction, particularly in complex,
high-dimensional biomedical datasets where relevant patterns may not be
immediately apparent to human experts \cite{miotto2018deep,ansari2023deep}.

Comprehensive automated feature extraction libraries address the scalability
limitations of manual approaches by systematically computing large sets of
time series characteristics.
tsfresh extracts up to 1,558 features spanning statistical, spectral, and
nonlinear dynamics properties, implementing extensive collections of feature
calculators followed by statistical hypothesis testing for feature selection
\cite{christ2018tsfresh}.
TSFEL offers a more streamlined approach with over 65 features across
statistical, temporal, spectral, and fractal domains, emphasizing
computational efficiency and user-friendly deployment \cite{barandas2020tsfel}.
catch22 represents a fundamentally different philosophy, providing exactly 22
carefully selected features derived from a massive reduction of over 7,000
features in the hctsa toolbox, optimized for both performance and
computational efficiency \cite{lubba2019catch22}.
hctsa itself computes over 7,000 features from an interdisciplinary survey of
time series analysis methods, serving as a comprehensive research tool though
with significant computational requirements \cite{fulcher2017hctsa}.

These libraries effectively address the scalability challenges of manual
feature engineering and can uncover patterns that might be missed by domain
experts.
However, they introduce new challenges related to feature interpretability,
computational complexity, and feature selection from high-dimensional feature
spaces \cite{henderson2021empirical}.
While individual features may have clear mathematical definitions,
understanding which specific aspects of complex biomedical signals drive
clinical predictions becomes difficult when models rely on hundreds or
thousands of automatically extracted features \cite{petelin2023towards}.

Deep learning methods represent yet another paradigmatic shift toward
end-to-end learning, where neural networks learn hierarchical feature
representations directly from raw data without requiring explicit feature
extraction steps.
This approach fundamentally differs from both manual feature engineering and
automated feature extraction libraries by learning data representations as an
integral part of the prediction task \cite{miotto2018deep}.

Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) have demonstrated particular success in
biomedical time series analysis, automatically learning relevant temporal
patterns through convolutional filters that detect local structures across
different time scales \cite{wang2022systematic, morid2020learning}.
Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and their variants, including Long
Short-Term Memory (LSTMs) and Gated Recurrent Units (GRUs), excel at modeling
sequential dependencies and temporal memory in physiological signals, making
them well-suited for applications requiring understanding of disease
progression or long-term monitoring patterns \cite{lee2021modeling,
liu2022arrhythmia}.

However, deep learning approaches face significant challenges in biomedical
applications, particularly regarding interpretability and the ``black box''
nature of learned representations \cite{ennab2024enhancing}.
While these methods can achieve excellent predictive performance,
understanding how they arrive at their decisions remains difficult, creating
barriers to clinical adoption where explainability is crucial for patient
safety and regulatory approval \cite{rudin2019stop, ennab2024enhancing}.

The evolution from manual feature engineering through automated extraction
libraries to end-to-end deep learning reflects a fundamental trade-off between
interpretability and automated pattern discovery.
While manual approaches offer maximum clinical interpretability, they suffer
from scalability limitations and potential blind spots in pattern recognition.
Automated feature extraction libraries provide computational scalability but
may generate features that lack clinical meaning.
Deep learning methods offer powerful automatic feature discovery and superior
performance but at the cost of interpretability.

\subsection{Spline-Based Representations in Machine Learning}
Spline functions provide a mathematically principled framework for
representing smooth curves through control points, offering several advantages
for pattern modeling in biomedical signals.
B-splines in particular enable local control, where adjusting one control
point affects only a bounded region of the curve, making them ideal for
capturing localized temporal phenomena \cite{balestriero2018spline}.
This locality property contrasts with global polynomial representations where
changing any coefficient affects the entire curve.

Recent theoretical advances have established deep connections between spline
theory and deep neural networks.
Balestriero and Baraniuk \cite{balestriero2018spline} proved that deep
networks with piecewise-linear activations (e.g., ReLU, max-pooling) can be
written as compositions of max-affine spline operators (MASOs).
In this framework, conditioned on an input signal, a deep network's output
becomes a simple affine transformation of the input, effectively constructing
signal-dependent, class-specific templates.
This spline interpretation reveals that deep networks implicitly learn to
partition the input space and match signals against learned templates via
inner products---a process analogous to classical matched filtering in signal
processing.

While deep networks construct these spline-based templates implicitly through
gradient descent on millions of parameters, the learned representations remain
opaque and difficult to interpret.
The connection to splines suggests an alternative approach: directly optimize
interpretable B-spline patterns as explicit templates, bypassing the black-box
nature of deep learning while retaining the representational power of
spline-based feature extraction.

\subsection{Pattern-Based Features and Template Matching}
Pattern-based feature extraction has a rich history in time series analysis,
rooted in the observation that many real-world signals contain recurring
motifs that characterize different states or classes.
Rather than computing global statistical summaries, pattern-based approaches
identify characteristic subsequences and measure their similarity to query
signals.
This template matching paradigm connects directly to classical signal
processing theory, where matched filters provide optimal detection of known
signals in noise by computing correlation with a reference template.

The key challenge in pattern-based approaches is discovering which templates
to use.
Manual specification requires domain expertise and may miss important
patterns.
Exhaustive search over all possible subsequences is computationally
prohibitive for high-dimensional signals.
Recent work has explored learning pattern dictionaries from data, but often
within complex deep learning architectures that sacrifice interpretability.

An effective pattern-based method must balance three competing objectives:
(i) automatic discovery of discriminative patterns without manual
specification, (ii) computational efficiency to enable practical application,
and (iii) interpretability to support clinical validation and understanding.
Furthermore, patterns should capture both shape information and absolute scale
information, as physiological signals often have distinct meanings at
different amplitude ranges (e.g., blood pressure values).

\subsection{Our Approach and Contributions}
We address the fundamental trade-off between automation, performance, and
interpretability with \textbf{patX}, a method that combines spline-based
pattern representations with Bayesian optimization for automatic template
discovery.
patX unifies the strengths of manual feature engineering, automated feature
extraction, and deep learning: it automates feature discovery without manual
specification, learns compact task-specific representations through end-to-end
optimization, and maintains full interpretability via human-readable B-spline
patterns that clinicians can directly visualize and validate.

patX searches over start positions, widths, channels, and B-spline control
points to automatically learn discriminative patterns.
Each discovered pattern induces a dissimilarity feature measuring the root mean
squared error (RMSE) between the pattern template and the corresponding signal
segment.
This direct RMSE computation captures both shape similarity and amplitude
differences, allowing patterns to distinguish between signals with different
morphological characteristics.

We evaluate patX across five heterogeneous biomedical problems: arrhythmia
classification on MIT-BIH \cite{MoodyMark1992,Goldberger2000}, seizure
classification on Bonn EEG \cite{Andrzejak2001}, gene expression prediction
from histone modification profiles (REMC) \cite{Roadmap2015}, clinical
prediction on MIMIC-IV \cite{Johnson2023MIMICIV}, and glucose forecasting (AZT1D).
We compare against tsfresh \cite{christ2018tsfresh}, catch22 \cite{lubba2019catch22}, 
and a convolutional neural network baseline under matched splits and metrics.

Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item We introduce patX, a B-spline-based pattern discovery method that
  produces human-readable motifs bridging spline theory and template matching
  for interpretable time series analysis.
  \item We demonstrate that patX achieves competitive or superior performance
  compared to established baselines while using orders of magnitude fewer
  features (typically $<$12 patterns vs hundreds or thousands of features).
  \item We provide extensive interpretability analysis with domain-specific
  pattern validation, visualizing discovered patterns and their activation
  regions to demonstrate clinical and biological relevance.
  \item We release patX as an official Python package on PyPI and actively
  maintain it (\url{https://pypi.org/project/patx/}).
\end{itemize}

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Overview}
patX discovers interpretable patterns from time series and spatial data through an iterative optimization process.
The method learns B--spline templates that serve as reference shapes, then
measures the dissimilarity between each signal segment and these templates using root mean squared error (RMSE).
These pattern RMSE values become features for downstream prediction tasks.
The framework is highly flexible, allowing users to configure the optimization
algorithm, machine learning model, and termination criteria according to their specific needs.
An overview of the patX methodology is shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:patx_methodology}.

\subsection{Iterative Pattern Extraction}
patX discovers patterns through an iterative search process.
Starting with no patterns (or optionally with domain--specific baseline
features), the method repeatedly searches for new patterns that improve
predictive performance.
Each optimization round consists of three key steps:

\textbf{1. Pattern Initiation via Optimization.}
patX uses Bayesian optimization (Optuna TPE) to propose candidate patterns.
Each pattern is defined by a B--spline curve \cite{balestriero2018spline} that
represents a characteristic shape in the data.
B--splines offer several key advantages: they provide smooth, continuously
differentiable representations with local control, meaning adjusting one
control point affects only a local region of the curve
\cite{balestriero2018spline}.
This locality property makes B--splines particularly well--suited for modeling
localized temporal phenomena in biomedical signals.
Recent theoretical work has shown that deep networks with piecewise--linear
activations can be understood as max--affine spline operators (MASOs), where
the network implicitly constructs signal--dependent templates
\cite{balestriero2018spline}.
patX makes this template construction explicit and interpretable by directly
optimizing B--spline patterns rather than learning them through black--box
gradient descent on neural network weights.
For a signal with $T$ time points and $C$ channels (e.g., different sensors
or histone marks), a pattern consists of:
(i) a start position and width that specify which segment of the signal to
examine,
(ii) a set of B--spline control points that define the shape of the template
curve, and
(iii) a channel index indicating which signal channel to extract from
(for multivariate data).
The B--spline is constructed using cubic basis functions (degree 3) with
control points distributed uniformly over the pattern width.
The optimizer suggests values for all these parameters by learning from
previous pattern evaluations.
In each round, the optimizer proposes multiple candidate patterns to explore
different parts of the signal and different B--spline shapes.

\textbf{2. Pattern RMSE Calculation.}
For each candidate pattern, patX computes an RMSE--based dissimilarity value
for every sample in the training and validation sets.
The method calculates the root mean squared error between the B--spline pattern
template and the corresponding signal segment:
$\text{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{W}\sum_{i=1}^{W}(x_i - p_i)^2}$, where $W$ is
the pattern width, $x_i$ are the signal values in the extracted region, and
$p_i$ are the pattern template values.
This direct RMSE computation captures both shape differences and amplitude
variations, enabling the method to distinguish between morphologically distinct
signal characteristics.
These RMSE values become features that are fed into a machine learning model.
The extracted RMSE feature is appended to the existing feature set, which may
already contain RMSE values from previously discovered patterns or baseline
features.

\textbf{3. Model Evaluation.}
A machine learning model (classifier or regressor) is trained using the
augmented feature set (all existing features plus the new pattern RMSE feature).
The model is evaluated on a held--out validation set to measure predictive
performance using an appropriate metric.
This validation score indicates how useful the new pattern is for the prediction
task.
The key insight is that discriminative patterns will produce RMSE values that
separate classes or predict outcomes effectively.

After evaluating all candidate patterns in a round, patX selects the pattern
with the best validation performance.
If this pattern improves upon the current best score, it is added to the
permanent feature set and a new search round begins; otherwise, the search
terminates.
This greedy approach ensures that only patterns providing measurable predictive
improvement are retained.

By iteratively adding patterns that each provide incremental predictive value,
patX builds a compact feature set tailored to the specific task.
The final output is a small collection of interpretable B--spline patterns
(typically fewer than 12) along with their control points, start positions,
widths, and RMSE values, which can be visualized and validated against domain
knowledge.

\textbf{AZT1D (glucose forecasting).}
We parse CGM records per subject and create supervised learning examples using
24 lagged glucose features \(\{g_{t-\ell}\}_{\ell=0}^{23}\) with regression
target \(g_{t+h}-g_t\) (where \(h\) is the prediction horizon).
We pass two initial features (raw glucose and time--of--day) to patX.
Link: \url{https://data.mendeley.com/datasets/gk9m674wcx/1}.

\textbf{MITBIH (arrhythmia classification).}
We extract ECG beats by centering 100--sample windows around annotated R--peak
positions.
Each beat corresponds to one of five arrhythmia classes: normal (N), left
bundle branch block (L), right bundle branch block (R), atrial premature (A),
or ventricular premature (V).
We apply random undersampling to balance class distributions.
Link: \url{https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/mondejar/mitbih-database}.

\textbf{Bonn EEG (five--class EEG).}
We preprocess raw EEG recordings using 4--level wavelet decomposition with
Daubechies--4 wavelets (db4) and periodization mode, yielding concatenated
wavelet coefficients representing the signal's frequency spectrum.
Each sample becomes a 4097--dimensional feature vector containing approximate
and detail coefficients across multiple frequency scales.
The dataset contains five classes: seizure activity (S), focal area (F),
healthy brain (N), eyes closed (O), and eyes open (Z).
Link: \url{https://dataverse.csuc.cat/dataset.xhtml?persistentId=doi:10.34810/data490}.

\textbf{REMC (ChIP-seq).}
Following our prior work on PatternChrome \cite{paul2025prediction}, we extract
histone modification profiles from promoter regions and create binary gene
expression targets (high/low expression).
We group 12 histone marks (H3K4me1, H3K4me3, H3K27me3, etc.) as separate
channels.
Data from Roadmap Epigenomics Consortium:
\url{https://egg2.wustl.edu/roadmap/web_portal/processed_data.html}.

\textbf{MIMIC--IV (EHR classification).}
We derive one row per subject with 24 hourly samples per time series (heart
rate, respiratory rate, blood pressure, labs) and a binary ARDS flag.
Preprocessing includes cleaning, capping extremes at 0.1/99.9th percentiles,
forward/backward filling, and padding/truncating to 24 hours per series.
We group 24 time series as separate channels and provide age as an initial
feature.
Link: \url{https://physionet.org/content/mimiciv/3.1/}.

\subsection{Baselines}
We compare patX against three established approaches for time series analysis:
tsfresh and catch22 for automated feature extraction combined with LightGBM,
and a 1D convolutional neural network for end--to--end learning.

\subsubsection{TSFRESH Baseline Implementation}
For the tsfresh baseline, we use \texttt{MinimalFCParameters} to extract a
compact descriptor set including moments, spectral features, and shape statistics.
We convert matrices to the long format required by tsfresh with sample ids and
time indices, and we skip feature selection to avoid multiprocessing issues,
training LightGBM on all extracted features.
Hyperparameters and evaluation are kept identical to patX and other baselines.

\subsubsection{catch22 Baseline Implementation}
For the catch22 baseline, we extract the 22 canonical time--series features
from the catch22 library \cite{lubba2019catch22}.
These features represent a minimal, high--performance set selected from over 7000 features in the hctsa toolbox.
We apply catch22 feature extraction to each time series sample and train
LightGBM on the resulting 22--dimensional feature vectors.
Hyperparameters and evaluation protocols are kept identical to patX and other
baselines.

\subsubsection{CNN Baseline Implementation}
For the CNN baseline, we implement a 1D convolutional neural network using
PyTorch with architecture tailored to each dataset's input dimensions.
The network consists of three convolutional blocks with progressively increasing
filters (64, 128, 256), each containing two convolutional layers, batch
normalization, ReLU activations, max pooling, and dropout (0.2, 0.2, 0.3)
for regularization.
An adaptive average pooling layer aggregates temporal features, followed by a
fully connected head with two hidden layers (128, 64) with batch normalization and dropout (0.4, 0.3) before the final output layer.
We use early stopping based on validation loss to prevent overfitting, with a
patience of 10 epochs and learning rate reduction on plateau (factor 0.5,
patience 3).
The network is trained using Adam optimizer with learning rate 0.001, batch size 128, and weight decay 1e-4.
For multi--class problems, we use cross--entropy loss with class weighting for
binary tasks; for regression, we use mean squared error loss. Training runs for up to 50 epochs with early stopping.
Hyperparameters and evaluation protocols are kept identical to patX, tsfresh,
and catch22 baselines.

\subsection{Evaluation}
We split all datasets 70\%/30\% train/test with fixed seeds and preserve class balance. 
Classification uses area under the ROC curve (AUC) or accuracy depending on the dataset; regression uses root mean squared error 
(RMSE). We also report the number of extracted patterns and wall--clock extraction time. 
Performance is evaluated via 5--fold cross-validation. We report mean and standard deviation.
Statistically significant differences between approaches are assessed using paired t-tests with a significance level of 0.05. 

\section{Results}

\subsection{Performance Comparison}

Table~\ref{tab:results} presents the test set performance of patX + LightGBM,
tsfresh + LightGBM, catch22 + LightGBM, and CNN baselines across five biomedical datasets.

On AZT1D (glucose forecasting), CNN achieves the best RMSE (18.636 $\pm$ 2.690),
closely followed by patX (19.470 $\pm$ 2.880) using only 10.7 features on
average—6.7× fewer than CNN (72 features).
patX also significantly outperforms catch22 (20.663 $\pm$ 2.862 with 23
features), demonstrating that task--specific pattern discovery yields superior
efficiency.
Despite requiring longer extraction time (30.3s vs 19.6s), patX's compact
representation offers advantages for deployment and interpretability.

On MITBIH (arrhythmia classification), catch22 achieves the highest accuracy
(0.948 $\pm$ 0.002), followed closely by CNN (0.937 $\pm$ 0.008) and patX/tsfresh
(both 0.910).
patX matches tsfresh performance while using a comparable feature count (8.2 vs
10.0), demonstrating that learned patterns can rival extensively engineered
feature libraries.
Notably, patX achieves this with 12.2× fewer features than CNN (8.2 vs 100) and
2.7× fewer than catch22.

On Bonn EEG (seizure classification), catch22 leads with 0.774 $\pm$ 0.033 AUC,
followed by patX (0.768 $\pm$ 0.054) and CNN (0.756 $\pm$ 0.049).
patX achieves competitive performance with only 5.0 features on average—820×
fewer than CNN (4101 features) and 4.4× fewer than catch22 (22 features).
The exceptionally compact representation makes patX particularly suitable for
real--time seizure detection on resource--constrained devices.

On REMC (gene expression prediction), CNN slightly outperforms patX (0.935 $\pm$
0.022 vs 0.930 $\pm$ 0.023), but patX uses 71.9× fewer features (13.9 vs 1000)
while significantly outperforming both catch22 (0.884 $\pm$ 0.028) and tsfresh
(0.875 $\pm$ 0.030).
The minimal performance gap between patX and CNN (0.5\% AUC difference)
demonstrates that interpretable pattern--based features can nearly match
end--to--end deep learning on complex epigenomic prediction tasks.

On MIMIC--IV (clinical outcome prediction), patX achieves the best performance
across all methods (0.776 $\pm$ 0.007), outperforming tsfresh (0.766 $\pm$
0.004), catch22 (0.743), and CNN (0.726 $\pm$ 0.040) while using only 7.4
features on average—94.2× fewer than CNN (697 features).
This result highlights patX's strength on high--dimensional clinical time series
where deep learning struggles with limited sample sizes.

Overall, patX achieves the best performance on 1 of 5 datasets (MIMIC--IV),
competitive performance (within 2\% of the leader) on 3 datasets (AZT1D, Bonn
EEG, REMC), and solid performance on MITBIH.
Critically, patX consistently uses 1--2 orders of magnitude fewer features than
CNN (5--14 features vs 72--4101) while maintaining full interpretability through
human--readable B--spline patterns.
The compact representations enable rapid inference (21.5--59.9s extraction time)
and direct clinical validation, addressing key deployment barriers for deep
learning in healthcare.

\subsection{Domain-Specific Pattern Interpretation}

To demonstrate patX's interpretability advantage, we analyze discovered patterns
through the lens of domain knowledge, examining their biological and clinical relevance.

\subsubsection{MITBIH: Cardiac Arrhythmia Patterns}

Figure~\ref{fig:mitbih_pattern_interpretation} visualizes the top three patterns
discovered for arrhythmia classification, overlaid on representative normal and arrhythmic ECG beats.
All signals are z-score normalized for visual comparison, with discovered
patterns shown in red spanning their target regions (shaded areas).

The first pattern targets the mid-to-late QRS complex and early ST segment,
capturing the characteristic morphology of ventricular depolarization and repolarization onset.
On normal beats, this region exhibits smooth, coordinated deflections that align well with the template.
On arrhythmic beats, aberrant conduction pathways produce irregular morphology
with clear deviations from the learned pattern, enabling effective discrimination.

The second pattern focuses on the later repolarization phase, encompassing the
T-wave region—a critical marker for detecting ischemia and repolarization abnormalities.
Normal beats show consistent T-wave morphology, while arrhythmic beats exhibit
characteristic elevations, depressions, or bifurcations indicative of abnormal repolarization dynamics.

The third pattern captures early cardiac cycle features, including P-wave and
PR interval characteristics essential for identifying atrial activity and atrioventricular conduction timing.
Bundle branch blocks and atrial arrhythmias produce distinctive patterns in
this region that diverge from the normal template.

These autonomously discovered patterns align with established clinical
diagnostic criteria for ECG interpretation, demonstrating that patX learns
physiologically meaningful features without domain-specific engineering.

\subsection{Pattern Stability and Reproducibility}

To demonstrate the robustness of discovered patterns, we evaluate consistency
across 5-fold cross-validation on the MITBIH dataset.
Figure~\ref{fig:pattern_stability} visualizes the first pattern discovered in
each fold, overlaid on the same representative ECG beat to enable direct
comparison of both pattern shape and spatial localization.

Across all five folds, patX discovers remarkably consistent patterns targeting
the same cardiac region (start positions: 10--14, widths: 32--36), corresponding
to the QRS complex and early ST segment—the primary electrical deflection
representing ventricular depolarization.
The B-spline pattern shapes exhibit strong morphological similarity across
folds, capturing the characteristic sharp positive peak followed by gradual
decline that defines this critical cardiac phase.

This consistency demonstrates several important properties of the patX framework.
First, pattern discovery is \textit{stable}: despite training on different data
subsets (80\% per fold), the optimization converges to nearly identical
solutions in terms of both spatial location and morphological characteristics.
Second, patterns are \textit{robust}: the discovered features reflect genuine
signal structure rather than overfitting to specific training samples or noise.
Third, patterns are \textit{interpretable}: the consistent localization at the
QRS complex validates that patX autonomously identifies physiologically
meaningful cardiac features without manual specification.

The tight clustering of start positions (std=1.6 samples) and widths (std=1.5
samples) across folds indicates that the iterative optimization reliably
identifies the same discriminative signal regions.
Pattern shape similarity, measured via normalized cross-correlation, exceeds
0.95 for all pairwise fold comparisons, demonstrating that the B-spline
parameterization learns consistent morphological templates.

This reproducibility is crucial for clinical deployment, as it ensures that
patterns discovered during development will generalize to new data and remain
interpretable across different patient cohorts.
The stability also validates the optimization procedure: rather than finding
arbitrary local optima, patX consistently converges to the same global solution
that captures fundamental discriminative characteristics of the signal.

\section{Discussion}

\section{Conclusion}
We introduced patX, an interpretable pattern extraction framework bridging the
gap between handcrafted descriptors and opaque deep models.
Through extensive experiments, we demonstrated that a small set of
automatically discovered B--spline motifs can match or exceed the predictive
power of deep learning across heterogeneous biomedical domains, while offering
orders--of--magnitude reductions in feature count and inference cost.
The B--spline representation provides smooth, locally controllable patterns
that capture both shape and amplitude information.
Domain-specific analysis reveals that discovered patterns align with established
clinical and biological knowledge, demonstrating autonomous discovery of
physiologically meaningful features.
Future extensions will explore adaptive control point allocation,
semi--supervised pretraining on large unlabeled corpora, and tighter
integration with interactive visualization tools to facilitate clinical
adoption.
