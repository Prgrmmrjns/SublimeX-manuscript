\begin{abstract}
We introduce \textbf{patX}, an open--source Python package that automatically 
discovers compact, highly predictive, and interpretable patterns from biomedical 
time--series and spatial data. By framing pattern discovery as a constrained 
optimization problem over a pool of candidate subsequences, patX learns 
human--readable patterns that serve as transparent input features for conventional 
machine--learning models. We demonstrate patX on four diverse biomedical tasks: 
(i) glucose forecasting (AZT1D), (ii) arrhythmia classification (MITBIH), 
(iii) seizure classification (Bonn EEG), and (iv) gene expression prediction 
from epigenetic signatures (REMC). Across four of the five tasks, patX outperforms tsfresh and convolutional neural networks, 
while providing direct interpretability and significant speed–ups at inference time.
We also demonstrate the interpretability of patX patterns by visualizing pattern and the area at which they are active.
Our tool is available at \url{https://github.com/Prgrmmrjns/patX} and offers a simple interface for extracting patterns from time series and spatial data.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% Abundance and importance of time series and spatial data in medicine
Biomedical research has witnessed an unprecedented expansion in the generation and utilization of high-resolution 
time series and spatial data across multiple domains. From continuous physiological monitoring through electrocardiography (ECG) 
and electroencephalography (EEG) to advanced spatially-resolved omics technologies, these data modalities have become fundamental 
to modern healthcare and biomedical discovery \cite{morid2023time, rood2022impact}.

Time series data in healthcare captures the temporal evolution of patient health, offering crucial insights into disease progression, 
treatment effectiveness, and physiological dynamics that static measurements cannot provide \cite{patharkar2024predictive}. 
Continuous monitoring systems such as smart watches or electroencephalography devices generate vast streams of physiological signals—from heart rate variability 
and blood pressure fluctuations to brain activity patterns—that contain rich information about both normal physiology and pathological states \cite{chaddad2023electroencephalography}.

% Applications of time series and spatial data in medicine
The clinical applications of these data types are extensive and growing. Electrocardiography (ECG) analysis supports arrhythmia detection and cardiovascular risk stratification, 
with modern systems processing thousands of heartbeats to identify subtle patterns indicative of cardiac pathology \cite{anbalagan2023analysis}. 
EEG monitoring enables real-time seizure detection, sleep stage classification, and assessment of neurological function across various conditions \cite{chaddad2023electroencephalography}. 
In genomics and epigenomics, spatial profiling of chromatin modifications and gene expression patterns provides 
insights into regulatory mechanisms underlying development and disease \cite{yadav2022systems}.

% Manual Feature Engineering
The analysis of biomedical time series and spatial data has evolved through three different paradigms that represent fundamentally different philosophies 
toward feature representation and model development. 
Traditional approaches to biomedical signal analysis rely heavily on domain expertise to manually craft features that capture relevant temporal and spatial characteristics. 
This paradigm involves extracting carefully designed statistical measures, temporal properties, spectral features, and morphological characteristics 
based on established understanding of physiological processes \cite{fulcher2018feature}. 
For ECG analysis, clinicians and engineers have developed features capturing heart rate variability, QRS complex morphology, and ST-segment deviations \cite{gelen2025novel, ansari2023deep}. 
Similarly, EEG analysis traditionally focuses on frequency band power, coherence measures, and event-related potentials derived from decades of neurophysiology research \cite{moutonnet2025augmentation}.

This approach benefits significantly from the incorporation of clinical domain knowledge and provides features with clear physiological interpretations that align with medical understanding. 
Manual feature engineering allows for precise targeting of known pathological markers and enables clinicians to understand exactly which signal characteristics drive model predictions \cite{gelen2025novel}. 

However, manual feature engineering suffers from several critical limitations that constrain its scalability and generalizability. 
The feature engineering process is labor-intensive, requiring expertise in both signal processing techniques and the specific clinical domain 
as well as requiring a preceding detailed exploratory data analysis \cite{roe2020feature}. 
This expertise requirement creates bottlenecks in model development and limits the ability to rapidly adapt to new clinical problems or signal types. 
Moreover, manually crafted features may miss subtle patterns that could be crucial for accurate classification or prediction, 
particularly in complex, high-dimensional biomedical datasets where relevant patterns may not be immediately apparent to human experts \cite{miotto2018deep,ansari2023deep}.

% Automated Feature Engineering Packages
Comprehensive automated feature extraction libraries address the scalability limitations of manual approaches by systematically computing large sets of time series characteristics. 
tsfresh extracts up to 1,558 features spanning statistical, spectral, and nonlinear dynamics properties, implementing extensive collections of feature calculators
followed by statistical hypothesis testing for feature selection \cite{christ2018tsfresh}. 
TSFEL offers a more streamlined approach with over 65 features across statistical, temporal, spectral, 
and fractal domains, emphasizing computational efficiency and user-friendly deployment \cite{barandas2020tsfel}. 
catch22 represents a fundamentally different philosophy, providing exactly 22 carefully selected features derived from a massive reduction of over 7,000 features in the hctsa toolbox, 
optimized for both performance and computational efficiency \cite{lubba2019catch22}. hctsa itself computes over 7,000 features from an interdisciplinary survey of time series analysis methods, 
serving as a comprehensive research tool though with significant computational requirements \cite{fulcher2017hctsa}.

These libraries effectively address the scalability challenges of manual feature engineering and can uncover patterns that might be missed by domain experts. 
However, they introduce new challenges related to feature interpretability, computational complexity, and feature selection from high-dimensional feature spaces \cite{henderson2021empirical}. 
While individual features may have clear mathematical definitions, understanding which specific aspects of complex biomedical signals drive clinical predictions 
becomes difficult when models rely on hundreds or thousands of automatically extracted features \cite{petelin2023towards}.

Deep learning methods represent yet another paradigmatic shift toward end-to-end learning, where neural networks learn hierarchical feature representations directly 
from raw data without requiring explicit feature extraction steps. This approach fundamentally differs from both manual feature engineering and automated feature extraction libraries 
by learning data representations as an integral part of the prediction task \cite{miotto2018deep}.

Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) have demonstrated particular success in biomedical time series analysis, automatically learning relevant temporal patterns through 
convolutional filters that detect local structures across different time scales \cite{wang2022systematic, morid2020learning}. 
Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and their variants, including Long Short-Term Memory (LSTMs) and Gated Recurrent Units (GRUs), 
excel at modeling sequential dependencies and temporal memory in physiological signals, 
making them well-suited for applications requiring understanding of disease progression or long-term monitoring patterns \cite{lee2021modeling, liu2022arrhythmia}.

However, deep learning approaches face significant challenges in biomedical applications, particularly regarding interpretability and the “black box” nature of learned representations \cite{ennab2024enhancing}. 
While these methods can achieve excellent predictive performance, understanding how they arrive at their decisions remains difficult, 
creating barriers to clinical adoption where explainability is crucial for patient safety and regulatory approval \cite{rudin2019stop, ennab2024enhancing}. 
The evolution from manual feature engineering through automated extraction libraries to end-to-end deep learning reflects a fundamental trade-off between interpretability and 
automated pattern discovery. While manual approaches offer maximum clinical interpretability, they suffer from scalability limitations and potential blind spots in pattern recognition. 
Automated feature extraction libraries provide computational scalability but may generate features that lack clinical meaning. 
Deep learning methods offer powerful automatic feature discovery and superior performance but at the cost of interpretability.

We address this trade-off with \textbf{patX}, a method that unifies the strengths of all three approaches: automatic feature extraction without manual engineering, end--to--end
representation learning that discovers task--relevant patterns from raw signals, and transparent interpretability through human--readable polynomial motifs rather than
black--box architectures. patX searches over start/end indices, channels, and polynomial shape parameters to automatically learn discriminative patterns. 
Each discovered pattern induces a similarity feature based on root mean squared error to the corresponding signal segment. 
By combining guided search with a lightweight learner for pattern evaluation, patX achieves three key objectives simultaneously: it automates feature discovery like
deep networks, learns compact representations optimized for the target task, and maintains full interpretability by producing inspectable patterns that clinicians can directly
visualize and validate against domain knowledge.

We evaluate patX across heterogeneous biomedical problems: arrhythmia classification on MIT--BIH \cite{MoodyMark1992,Goldberger2000}, seizure classification on Bonn EEG
\cite{Andrzejak2001}, gene expression prediction from histone modification profiles (REMC) \cite{Roadmap2015}, and clinical prediction on MIMIC--IV \cite{Johnson2023MIMICIV}, 
as well as a glucose forecasting task (AZT1D). We compare against tsfresh \cite{christ2018tsfresh} and a convolutional neural network baseline under matched splits and metrics.

Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item We introduce patX, a dynamic polynomial pattern search that produces human--readable motifs and associated similarity features 
  showcasing its strong performance on selected tasks against established baselines.
  \item We demonstrate the favorable interpretability of \texttt{patX} patterns by visualizing them and the areas at which they are active.
  \item We release patX as an official Python package on PyPI and actively maintain it (link: \url{https://pypi.org/project/patx/}).
\end{itemize}

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Overview}
patX discovers interpretable patterns from time series and spatial data through an iterative optimization process. 
Given input signals, patX searches for polynomial templates that, when compared to signal segments via root mean 
squared error (RMSE), produce discriminative features for downstream prediction tasks. The method combines 
Bayesian optimization for efficient search with gradient boosting for pattern evaluation, ultimately producing 
a small set of human--interpretable patterns.

\subsection{Pattern Representation}
Consider a signal $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times C}$ with $T$ time points and $C$ channels 
(for univariate signals, $C=1$). A pattern $p$ in patX is defined as a polynomial function over a 
contiguous interval:

\begin{equation}
p(t) = \sum_{i=0}^{d} c_i \cdot t^i, \quad t \in [-1, 1]
\end{equation}

where $d$ is the polynomial degree (default 3) and $\mathbf{c} = \{c_0, c_1, ..., c_d\}$ are the 
coefficients. Each pattern is characterized by:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Start position $s$ and width $w$ defining the interval $[s, s+w)$ in the signal
  \item Polynomial coefficients $\mathbf{c}$ defining the pattern shape
  \item Channel index $k$ (for multivariate signals) specifying which channel to extract from
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Feature Extraction}
For each pattern $p_j$, we compute a similarity feature by measuring the RMSE between the pattern 
template and the corresponding signal segment:

\begin{equation}
f_j(\mathbf{x}) = \sqrt{\frac{1}{w_j} \sum_{i=0}^{w_j-1} \left( x_{s_j+i,k_j} - p_j\left(\frac{2i}{w_j-1} - 1\right) \right)^2}
\end{equation}

where $x_{s_j+i,k_j}$ is the signal value at position $s_j+i$ in channel $k_j$. Before computing 
RMSE, we apply z--score normalization to both the signal segment and pattern template to ensure 
scale invariance.

\subsection{Optimization Process}
patX employs an iterative optimization strategy to discover patterns:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Initialization}: Start with an empty pattern set $\mathcal{P} = \emptyset$ and 
        optionally include domain--specific initial features (e.g., raw signal values)
  
  \item \textbf{Pattern Proposal}: At iteration $t$, use Bayesian optimization (Optuna with 
        Tree--Parzen Estimator) to propose $B$ candidate patterns by sampling from the search space:
        \begin{equation}
        \theta = (s, w, \mathbf{c}, k) \in [0,T) \times [w_{min}, w_{max}] \times \mathbb{R}^{d+1} \times [0,C)
        \end{equation}
  
  \item \textbf{Feature Matrix Construction}: For each candidate pattern, compute features 
        $\mathbf{F} = [f_1(\mathbf{x}), ..., f_j(\mathbf{x})]$ across all training samples
  
  \item \textbf{Pattern Evaluation}: Train a LightGBM classifier/regressor using current features 
        plus the candidate pattern feature. Evaluate on validation set to obtain performance metric 
        $\mathcal{L}_{val}$
  
  \item \textbf{Pattern Selection}: Accept the candidate with best validation performance if it 
        improves upon the current best score:
        \begin{equation}
        \mathcal{P}_{t+1} = \begin{cases}
        \mathcal{P}_t \cup \{p^*\} & \text{if } \mathcal{L}_{val}(p^*) > \mathcal{L}_{best} \\
        \mathcal{P}_t & \text{otherwise}
        \end{cases}
        \end{equation}
  
  \item \textbf{Termination}: Stop when no improvement for $N_{patience}$ iterations or maximum 
        iterations reached
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Implementation Details}
The core implementation uses the \texttt{PatternExtractor} class which:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Converts inputs to float32 NumPy arrays with shape $(N, C, T)$ for $N$ samples
  \item Caches computed features to avoid redundant calculations
  \item Supports parallel pattern evaluation across multiple CPU threads
  \item Exports discovered patterns to JSON for reproducibility
  \item Integrates with scikit--learn API for seamless pipeline integration
\end{itemize}

Typical hyperparameters include 50--200 optimization rounds with 50--200 candidate patterns per 
round, targeting final pattern sets of $\leq 12$ patterns to maintain interpretability.

\subsection{Data Pre--processing}
\textbf{AZT1D (glucose forecasting).} We parse CGM records per subject and create supervised learning 
examples using lagged glucose features. Specifically, we construct 24 lag variables 
\(\{g_{t-\ell}\}_{\ell=0}^{23}\) and define the regression target as the \(h\)--step change 
\(g_{t+h}-g_t\) (where \(h\) is the prediction horizon). We drop rows with missing values induced by 
shifts. In addition, we pass two initial features 
to patX (raw glucose and time--of--day) so that patterns can augment a simple contextual baseline. No 
scaling is applied beyond NaN filtering. Link: \url{https://data.mendeley.com/datasets/gk9m674wcx/1}.

\textbf{MITBIH (arrhythmia classification).} We extract ECG beats from raw MIT--BIH 
recordings by centering 100--sample windows around annotated R--peak positions. Each beat 
corresponds to one of five arrhythmia classes: normal (N), left bundle branch block (L), 
right bundle branch block (R), atrial premature (A), or ventricular premature (V). We 
apply random undersampling to balance class distributions. This dataset originates from the PhysioNet MIT--BIH Arrhythmia Database 
(link: \url{https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/mondejar/mitbih-database}).

\textbf{Bonn EEG (five--class EEG).} We preprocess raw EEG recordings using wavelet decomposition 
with PyWavelets (pywt). Each signal undergoes 4--level wavelet decomposition using Daubechies--4 
wavelets with periodization mode, yielding concatenated wavelet coefficients as features. The 
dataset contains five classes: seizure activity (S), focal area (F), healthy brain (N), 
eyes closed (O), and eyes open (Z). 
Link: \url{https://dataverse.csuc.cat/dataset.xhtml?persistentId=doi:10.34810/data490}.

\textbf{REMC (ChIP-seq).} We follow the same preprocessing approach as our prior work 
on PatternChrome \cite{paul2025prediction}. For each cell line, we extract histone 
modification profiles from promoter regions and create binary gene expression targets 
(high/low expression). We form multi--series inputs by grouping histone marks 
(H3K4me1, H3K4me3, H3K27me3, etc.) as separate channels. patX searches over 
start/width/polynomial coefficients per histone mark to discover predictive modification 
patterns within promoter regions. Performance is measured via AUC. Data from Roadmap 
Epigenomics Consortium: \url{https://egg2.wustl.edu/roadmap/web_portal/processed_data.html}.
Link of processed data: \url{https://gitlab.gwdg.de/MedBioinf/generegulation/patternchrome}

\textbf{MIMIC--IV (EHR classification).} We preprocess MIMIC--IV to derive one row per subject with 24 
hourly samples per time series (e.g., heart rate, respiratory rate, blood pressure, labs) and a binary 
ARDS flag. Preprocessing includes: cleaning required columns, 
sorting by subject and hour, replacing infinities, capping extremes at the 0.1/99.9th percentiles, 
forward/backward filling within subjects, and padding/truncating to 24 hours per series. We rename series 
to clean identifiers (e.g., hourly heart rate 0..23). We then group columns by series name to create a 
multi--series input list, standardize each series on the train 
split, and provide age as an initial feature (standardized) to patX. Link: \url{https://physionet.org/content/mimiciv/3.1/}.

\subsection{Datasets}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{AZT1D}: CGM and insulin pump data for type~1 diabetes forecasting (15/30~min horizons)
  \item \textbf{MITBIH}: ECG rhythm classification using the MIT--BIH Arrhythmia Database
  \item \textbf{Bonn EEG}: Five-class EEG dataset (Z,O,N,F,S) used in seizure research
  \item \textbf{REMC}: Histone modification profiles with gene expression labels from Roadmap 
        Epigenomics
  \item \textbf{MIMIC--IV}: EHR time series for clinical prediction tasks
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Baselines}
We compare patX against two established approaches for time series analysis. The first 
baseline uses tsfresh for automated feature extraction combined with LightGBM, while the 
second employs a 1D convolutional neural network for end--to--end learning.

\subsubsection{TSFRESH Baseline Implementation}
For the tsfresh baseline, we use \texttt{MinimalFCParameters} to extract a compact descriptor set 
including moments, spectral features, and shape statistics. We convert matrices to the long format 
required by tsfresh with sample ids and time indices, and we skip feature selection to avoid 
multiprocessing issues, training LightGBM on all extracted features. Hyperparameters and evaluation are 
kept identical to patX and other baselines.

\subsubsection{CNN Baseline Implementation}
For the CNN baseline, we implement a 1D convolutional neural network using PyTorch with 
architecture tailored to each dataset's input dimensions. The network consists of three 
convolutional layers with ReLU activations and max pooling, followed by dropout layers 
for regularization and fully connected layers for classification or regression. We use 
early stopping based on validation loss to prevent overfitting, with a patience of 10 
epochs. The network is trained using Adam optimizer with learning rate 0.001 and batch 
size 32. For multi--class problems, we use cross--entropy loss; for regression, we use 
mean squared error loss. Hyperparameters and evaluation protocols are kept identical 
to patX and tsfresh baselines.

\subsection{Evaluation}
We split all datasets 75\%/25\% train/test with fixed seeds and preserve class balance. 
Classification uses area under the ROC curve (AUC) or accuracy; regression uses root mean squared error 
(RMSE). We also report the number of extracted patterns and wall--clock extraction time. Where 
applicable, we compute 95\% CIs across seeds. Pattern search uses only training data. LightGBM is tuned 
via 5--fold CV on training features and retrained on the full train split. Results are averaged across 
3 seeds. We fix the total pattern budget to ensure comparable runtime across datasets and perform 
ablations on degree, window length, and similarity.
All baselines share the same data splits and evaluation protocol for a fair comparison.

On AZT1D (regression), CNN yields the lowest RMSE, while patX uses far fewer features and
achieves lower inference time than CNN. On MITBIH and Bonn EEG, CNN leads in accuracy/AUC,
but it requires orders of magnitude more features than patX (hundreds to thousands versus

On REMC and MIMIC--IV, patX achieves the best performance while using a small number of
patterns. In REMC, patX outperforms both tsfresh and CNN with roughly single--digit
patterns and competitive runtime. In MIMIC--IV, patX attains the highest accuracy/AUC
with a handful of features, whereas CNN uses considerably more learned filters.

Overall, the results show a consistent trade--off: CNN can be strong on some tasks when
large learned representations are allowed, but patX reaches competitive or superior
performance with drastically fewer features and favorable wall--clock times, while
providing human--readable patterns for interpretation.

\subsection{Decision Boundary Visualization}
We study a two--pattern decision boundary for REMC E003: we compute the RMSE
feature for the first two extracted patterns for each sample and fit a small gradient
boosting classifier (LightGBM) on this 2D feature space. Using 18,421 samples with a
75/25 train/test split, we achieve 76.4\% training accuracy and 74.6\% test accuracy
on the balanced binary classification task (2,304 vs 2,302 samples per class).

Figure~\ref{fig:remc_two_pattern_scatter} visualizes the decision boundary in the
two--dimensional feature space of RMSE values. Pattern 1 (start=44, width=55) yields
RMSE values ranging from 0.797 to 2.761, while Pattern 2 (start=12, width=94) produces
a tighter range from 0.383 to 2.074. The background regions show the predicted class
probabilities, with semi--transparent data points (X markers) overlaid to reveal the
actual class labels. This visualization demonstrates how just two interpretable pattern
features can effectively separate gene expression classes while remaining directly
inspectable and clinically meaningful.

\section{Discussion}

\section{Conclusion}
We introduced patX, an interpretable pattern extraction framework bridging the gap between handcrafted 
descriptors and opaque deep models. Through extensive experiments, we demonstrated that a small set of 
automatically discovered motifs can match or exceed the predictive power of deep learning across 
heterogeneous biomedical domains, while offering orders--of--magnitude reductions in feature count and 
inference cost. Future extensions will explore adaptive pattern lengths, semi--supervised pretraining on 
large unlabeled corpora, and tighter integration with interactive visualization tools to facilitate 
clinical adoption.
