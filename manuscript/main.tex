\title{patX: Interpretable Pattern Extraction for Time Series and Spatial Data}


\begin{document}

\begin{abstract}
We introduce \textbf{patX}, an open--source Python package that bridges the gap
between the automatic pattern learning capabilities of convolutional neural
networks (CNNs) and the interpretability requirements of biomedical applications.
Like CNNs, patX automatically discovers discriminative patterns from input data
and uses them for prediction; however, unlike CNNs where learned patterns remain
opaque, patX makes this pattern learning explicit and interpretable through
human--readable B--spline templates.
By framing pattern discovery as a constrained optimization problem over
B--spline templates across multiple signal transformations (raw, wavelet, FFT,
derivative), patX learns compact, highly predictive patterns that serve as
transparent input features for conventional machine--learning models.
We demonstrate patX on five diverse biomedical tasks: (i) glucose forecasting
(AZT1D), (ii) arrhythmia classification (MITBIH), (iii) emotion classification
from EEG signals (Emotions), (iv) gene expression prediction from epigenetic
signatures (REMC), and (v) acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS)
classification in an intensive care unit (MIMIC--IV).
Across these tasks, patX achieves best performance on three of five datasets compared to tsfresh, catch22, and CNN while
usually using fewer features and providing complete interpretability through explicit pattern visualization and validation.
Beyond predictive performance, patX's interpretable pattern discovery can lead
to novel scientific insights: from histone modification data, it can reveal
which modifications are most informative at specific genomic positions; from
ECG data, spectral features may uncover potential electrophysiological causes
of arrhythmias; and from glucose data, different temporal patterns can reveal
the relative importance of short-term versus long-term dynamics for different
patient populations.
This transparent approach to automatic feature extraction addresses the
``black box'' criticism of deep learning while maintaining competitive
performance, making patX particularly suitable for clinical applications where
explainability is crucial.
Our tool is available at \url{https://github.com/Prgrmmrjns/patX} and offers a
simple interface for extracting interpretable patterns from time series and
spatial data.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Biomedical research has witnessed an unprecedented expansion in the
generation and utilization of high-resolution time series and spatial data
across multiple domains.
From continuous physiological monitoring through electrocardiography (ECG)
and electroencephalography (EEG) to advanced spatially-resolved omics
technologies, these data modalities have become fundamental to modern
healthcare and biomedical discovery \cite{morid2023time, rood2022impact}.

Time series data in healthcare captures the temporal evolution of patient
health, offering crucial insights into disease progression, treatment
effectiveness, and physiological dynamics \cite{patharkar2024predictive}.
Continuous monitoring systems generate vast streams of physiological
signals---from ECG and EEG to glucose monitoring---that contain rich information
about both normal physiology and pathological states
\cite{chaddad2023electroencephalography, anbalagan2023analysis}.
In genomics, spatial profiling of chromatin modifications provides insights into
regulatory mechanisms underlying development and disease \cite{yadav2022systems}.

The analysis of biomedical time series and spatial data has evolved through
three different paradigms that represent fundamentally different philosophies
toward feature representation and model development.
Traditional approaches to biomedical signal analysis rely heavily on domain
expertise to manually craft features capturing relevant temporal and spatial
characteristics \cite{fulcher2018feature}.
For ECG analysis, features include heart rate variability and QRS complex
morphology \cite{gelen2025novel, ansari2023deep}; for EEG, frequency band power
and coherence measures \cite{moutonnet2025augmentation}.
While providing clear physiological interpretations, this approach is
labor-intensive, requires extensive domain expertise, and may miss subtle
patterns in complex datasets \cite{miotto2018deep,ansari2023deep}.

Comprehensive automated feature extraction libraries address scalability by
systematically computing large feature sets.
tsfresh extracts up to 1,558 features \cite{christ2018tsfresh}, while catch22
provides 22 carefully selected features optimized for performance and efficiency
\cite{lubba2019catch22}.
While effective at uncovering patterns, these libraries introduce challenges
related to interpretability and feature selection from high-dimensional spaces
\cite{henderson2021empirical, petelin2023towards}.

Deep learning methods learn hierarchical feature representations directly from
raw data through end-to-end training \cite{miotto2018deep}.
CNNs automatically learn temporal patterns through convolutional filters
\cite{wang2022systematic}, while RNNs and LSTMs model sequential dependencies
\cite{lee2021modeling}.
However, these approaches face significant interpretability challenges, creating
barriers to clinical adoption where explainability is crucial
\cite{rudin2019stop, ennab2024enhancing}.

This evolution reflects a fundamental trade-off between interpretability and
automated pattern discovery: manual approaches offer interpretability but lack
scalability, automated libraries provide scalability but reduce interpretability,
and deep learning offers performance but sacrifices explainability.

B-splines provide smooth curve representations with local control, where
adjusting one control point affects only a bounded region
\cite{balestriero2018spline}.
Recent work shows deep networks with piecewise-linear activations implicitly
construct spline-based templates through gradient descent, but these
representations remain opaque \cite{balestriero2018spline}.
This connection suggests directly optimizing interpretable B-spline patterns as
explicit templates, bypassing deep learning's black-box nature while retaining
spline-based representational power.

Pattern-based approaches identify characteristic subsequences and measure
similarity to query signals, connecting to matched filtering in signal
processing theory.
The key challenge is discovering templates automatically: manual specification
requires expertise, exhaustive search is prohibitive, and recent deep learning
approaches sacrifice interpretability.
An effective method must balance automatic discovery, computational efficiency,
and interpretability, while capturing both shape and amplitude information.

We address the fundamental trade-off between automation, performance, and
interpretability with \textbf{patX}, a method that combines spline-based
pattern representations with Bayesian optimization for automatic template
discovery.
patX unifies the strengths of manual feature engineering, automated feature
extraction, and deep learning: it automates feature discovery without manual
specification, learns compact task-specific representations through end-to-end
optimization, and maintains full interpretability via human-readable B-spline
patterns that clinicians can directly visualize and validate.

A key innovation of patX is its multi-representation search strategy.
Rather than operating solely on raw signals, patX precomputes multiple signal
transformations---including wavelet decompositions, Fourier transforms, and
derivatives---and automatically selects the most discriminative representation
for each pattern during optimization.
This allows patterns to match signals in the time domain, frequency domain,
or derivative space, capturing diverse signal characteristics that would
require manual specification in traditional feature engineering approaches.

patX searches over start positions, widths, channels, B-spline control
points, temporal shifts, pattern scaling modes (absolute vs. relative), and
signal transformations to automatically learn discriminative patterns.
Each discovered pattern induces a dissimilarity feature measuring the root mean
squared error (RMSE) between the pattern template and the corresponding
transformed signal segment, with temporal shifts allowing for robust matching
of misaligned patterns.
The optimization process also determines whether to match a pattern's absolute
values---capturing both shape and amplitude---or its relative shape, enabling
patX to distinguish between a wider range of morphological characteristics.

We evaluate patX across five heterogeneous biomedical problems: arrhythmia
classification on MIT-BIH \cite{MoodyMark1992,Goldberger2000}, emotion
classification from EEG signals (Emotions), gene expression prediction
from histone modification profiles (REMC) \cite{Roadmap2015}, clinical
prediction on MIMIC-IV \cite{Johnson2023MIMICIV} (ARDS classification), and glucose forecasting (AZT1D).
We compare against tsfresh \cite{christ2018tsfresh}, catch22 \cite{lubba2019catch22}, 
and a convolutional neural network baseline under matched splits and metrics.

We introduce patX, a B-spline-based pattern discovery method that
produces human-readable motifs bridging spline theory and template matching
for interpretable time series analysis, with automatic selection of signal
representations across multiple transformations (time, frequency, derivative).
We demonstrate that patX achieves competitive or superior performance
compared to established baselines on five diverse biomedical datasets,
winning on two and remaining competitive on three, while using orders of
magnitude fewer features (typically $<$14 patterns vs hundreds or thousands
of features).
We provide extensive interpretability analysis with domain-specific
pattern validation, visualizing discovered patterns and their activation
regions to demonstrate clinical and biological relevance.
We release patX as an official Python package on PyPI: (\url{https://pypi.org/project/patx/}).

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Overview}
patX discovers interpretable patterns from time series and spatial data through an iterative optimization process.
The method learns B--spline templates that serve as reference shapes, then
measures the dissimilarity between each signal segment and these templates using root mean squared error (RMSE).
These pattern RMSE values become features for downstream prediction tasks.

Before pattern optimization begins, patX precomputes multiple representations
of the input signals.
For each signal, the method generates five different views: the raw time--series
data, cumulative sum, moving average, FFT power spectra, and the temporal derivative.
These transformations are computed once and cached, enabling efficient pattern
search across multiple signal representations without repeated transformation
overhead.
During optimization, each candidate pattern automatically selects which
transformation provides the most discriminative features for the prediction task.

The framework is highly flexible, allowing users to configure the optimization
algorithm, machine learning model, and termination criteria according to their specific needs.
An overview of the patX methodology is shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:patx_methodology}.

\subsection{Iterative Pattern Extraction}
patX discovers patterns through an iterative search process.
Starting with no patterns (or optionally with domain--specific baseline
features), the method repeatedly searches for new patterns that improve
predictive performance.
Each optimization round consists of three key steps:

\textbf{1. Pattern Initiation via Optimization.}
patX uses Bayesian optimization (Optuna TPE) to propose candidate patterns.
Each pattern is defined by a B--spline curve \cite{balestriero2018spline} that
represents a characteristic shape in the data.
B--splines offer several key advantages: they provide smooth, continuously
differentiable representations with local control, meaning adjusting one
control point affects only a local region of the curve
\cite{balestriero2018spline}.
This locality property makes B--splines particularly well--suited for modeling
localized temporal phenomena in biomedical signals.
Recent theoretical work has shown that deep networks with piecewise--linear
activations can be understood as max--affine spline operators (MASOs), where
the network implicitly constructs signal--dependent templates
\cite{balestriero2018spline}.
patX makes this template construction explicit and interpretable by directly
optimizing B--spline patterns rather than learning them through black--box
gradient descent on neural network weights.
For a signal with $T$ time points and $C$ channels (e.g., different sensors
or histone marks), a pattern consists of:
(i) a start position and width that specify which segment of the signal to
examine,
(ii) a set of B--spline control points that define the shape of the template
curve,
(iii) a channel index indicating which signal channel to extract from (for
multivariate data),
(iv) a temporal shift tolerance that allows time--domain patterns (raw and
derivative transformations) to be matched flexibly within a window around their
nominal start position, while frequency--domain patterns (FFT and wavelet) match
at exact positions,
(v) a pattern mode (relative or absolute) that determines how B--spline control
point values are interpreted, and
(vi) a transformation type that selects which signal representation to match
against.
Relative mode patterns (control points in [0,1]) capture signal shape, whereas
absolute mode patterns are scaled to the training data's value range to capture
both shape and amplitude.
The transformation type determines which precomputed signal view to use:
\textit{raw} (original time--series), \textit{cumsum} (cumulative sum),
\textit{moving\_average} (smoothed signal), \textit{fft\_power} (frequency--domain 
power spectra), or \textit{derivative} (temporal rate of change via gradient).
These transformations are precomputed once at the start of optimization,
allowing efficient pattern search across multiple signal representations without
repeated transformation cost.
Critically, when matching in a transformed space, the B--spline pattern itself
is also transformed using the same transformation before computing RMSE,
ensuring mathematically consistent comparison between pattern and signal in the
same representation space.
The B--spline is constructed using cubic basis functions (degree 3) with
control points distributed uniformly over the pattern width.
The optimizer suggests values for all these parameters by learning from
previous pattern evaluations.
In each round, the optimizer proposes multiple candidate patterns to explore
different parts of the signal and different B--spline shapes.

\textbf{2. Pattern RMSE Calculation.}
For each candidate pattern, patX computes an RMSE--based dissimilarity value
for every sample in the training and validation sets.
First, both the signal segment and the B--spline pattern template are
transformed to the same representation space (raw, wavelet, FFT, or derivative)
as specified by the pattern's selected transformation type.
Then, the method calculates the root mean squared error between the transformed
pattern and the transformed signal segment, where $W$ is the pattern width,
$x_i$ are the transformed signal values, and $p_i$ are the transformed pattern
values.
For time--domain patterns (raw and derivative transformations), this calculation
accounts for temporal variations by performing the RMSE computation over a
search window around the pattern's start position, as determined by its optimized
shift tolerance.
The method evaluates the pattern at multiple positions within this window using
an adaptive stride mechanism that automatically scales with the search space
size to maintain computational efficiency.
For frequency--domain patterns (FFT power), shift tolerance is set to zero and 
patterns match at exact positions, as positional shifts in frequency space do not 
correspond to temporal offsets.
Specifically, the stride is computed as $\text{stride} = \max(1, W_{\text{search}}
/ N_{\text{max}})$, where $W_{\text{search}}$ is the window size and
$N_{\text{max}}$ is the maximum number of positions to evaluate (set to 50 in
our experiments).
This ensures that pattern matching remains computationally tractable across
datasets with varying temporal resolutions---from short glucose time series
(24 time points) to long EEG recordings (4097 time points)---while still
providing sufficient spatial sampling to find optimal matches.
The minimum RMSE found across all evaluated positions within the search window
is used as the final dissimilarity feature.
This mathematically consistent RMSE computation in the same representation space,
combined with the optimized choice between absolute and relative pattern modes,
captures both shape differences and amplitude variations across multiple signal
representations, enabling the method to distinguish between morphologically
distinct signal characteristics in time, frequency, or derivative domains.
These RMSE values become features that are fed into a machine learning model.
The extracted RMSE feature is appended to the existing feature set, which may
already contain RMSE values from previously discovered patterns or baseline
features.

\textbf{3. Model Evaluation.}
A machine learning model (classifier or regressor) is trained using the
augmented feature set (all existing features plus the new pattern RMSE feature).
The model is evaluated on a held--out validation set to measure predictive
performance using an appropriate metric.
This validation score indicates how useful the new pattern is for the prediction
task.
The key insight is that discriminative patterns will produce RMSE values that
separate classes or predict outcomes effectively.

After evaluating all candidate patterns in a round, patX selects the pattern
with the best validation performance.
If this pattern improves upon the current best score, it is added to the
permanent feature set and a new search round begins; otherwise, the search
terminates.
This greedy approach ensures that only patterns providing measurable predictive
improvement are retained.

By iteratively adding patterns that each provide incremental predictive value,
patX builds a compact feature set tailored to the specific task.
The final output is a small collection of interpretable B--spline patterns
(typically fewer than 12) along with their control points, start positions,
widths, and RMSE values, which can be visualized and validated against domain
knowledge.

\textbf{AZT1D (glucose forecasting).}
We parse continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) records per subject and create
supervised learning examples with 24 lagged timepoints (2 hours history at
5-minute intervals) for three physiological signals: CGM, insulin, and carbs.
The regression target is future glucose change $g_{t+h}-g_t$ where $h=30$
minutes.
A critical challenge in glucose forecasting is modeling delayed physiological
responses: insulin takes 10--90 minutes to affect glucose levels, while
carbohydrates impact glucose within 10--60 minutes, with effects persisting for
2--4 hours \cite{woldaregay2019data}.
We address this by computing active insulin and carbohydrate effects at each
timepoint using physiological response curves that model onset, peak, and
duration dynamics based on established pharmacokinetic and glycemic response
literature.
For insulin, we use a gamma-like function with 15-minute onset, 60-minute peak,
and 4-hour duration; for carbs, 10-minute onset, 45-minute peak, and 3-hour
duration.
These response curves convolve with event times and amounts to compute cumulative
active effects, creating interpretable time series that capture overlapping
insulin doses and meal intakes (see Supplementary Figure~\ref{fig:azt1d_preprocessing}).
We pass initial CGM value (CGM\_0) as baseline feature to patX.
Link: \url{https://data.mendeley.com/datasets/gk9m674wcx/1}.

\textbf{MITBIH (arrhythmia classification).}
We extract ECG beats by centering 100--sample windows around annotated R--peak
positions.
Each beat corresponds to one of five arrhythmia classes: normal (N), left
bundle branch block (L), right bundle branch block (R), atrial premature (A),
or ventricular premature (V).
We apply random undersampling to balance class distributions.
Link: \url{https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/mondejar/mitbih-database}.

\textbf{Emotions (emotion classification from EEG).}
We use EEG recordings from the emotions dataset containing brainwave data
collected while participants experienced different emotional states.
Each sample contains 254 time points across 5 EEG channels (AF3, F7, F3, FC5, T7).
The dataset contains three emotion classes: positive, neutral, and negative.
We allow patX to automatically select appropriate signal transformations
(including wavelet decompositions) as needed for pattern discovery.
Link: \url{https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/birdy654/eeg-brainwave-dataset-feeling-emotions}.

\textbf{REMC (ChIP-seq).}
Following our prior work on PatternChrome \cite{paul2025prediction}, we predict
binary gene expression (high/low) from histone modification ChIP-seq profiles.
The dataset contains gene expression data for 19,795 genes across 56 cell lines
from the Roadmap Epigenomics Consortium.
We convert RefSeq gene annotations to GENCODE identifiers, retaining 18,421
genes with successful mapping (1,374 genes lost).
For each gene, we extract five histone modification signals (H3K4me1, H3K4me3,
H3K36me3, H3K9me3, H3K27me3) from the region 5000 bp upstream and downstream of
the transcription start site (TSS).
This 10,000 bp region is divided into 50 bp bins, yielding 200 bins per histone
mark (1000 total features per gene).
Gene expression is binarized using the median expression level as threshold:
genes above the median are labeled as highly expressed, genes below as lowly
expressed.
We randomly sample 6,600 genes for training, 5,911 for validation, and 5,910
for testing, ensuring balanced labels across splits.
The five histone marks serve as separate input channels for patX.
Data from Roadmap Epigenomics Consortium:
\url{https://egg2.wustl.edu/roadmap/web_portal/processed_data.html}.

\textbf{MIMIC--IV (EHR classification).}
We predict acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS) from multivariate clinical
time series in intensive care unit patients \cite{Johnson2023MIMICIV}.
The dataset contains 29 physiological and clinical time series features per
subject, including vital signs (respiratory rate, heart rate, O2 saturation,
blood pressure, temperature), ventilator settings (FiO2, PEEP), Glasgow Coma
Scale scores, laboratory values (arterial pH, PaO2, creatinine, platelets, WBC,
INR, lactate, bilirubin), vasopressor medications (norepinephrine,
phenylephrine, epinephrine, dopamine, dobutamine), urine output (Foley), and
derived features (PaO2/FiO2 ratio, pulse pressure).
We extract 24-hour time series data from the first day of mechanical
ventilation in the ICU, binned into hourly intervals, with sequences
standardized through padding or truncation.
Missing values are imputed using forward and backward filling within each
subject's time series.
Extreme values are capped at the 0.1/99.9th percentiles to prevent scaling
issues.
Subjects with fewer than 5 hours of data are excluded.
The binary ARDS classification target is derived from diagnostic codes, with
patient age (anchor\_age) provided as an initial baseline feature.
The 29 time series are grouped as separate input channels for patX.
Link: \url{https://physionet.org/content/mimiciv/3.1/}.

\subsection{Baselines}
We compare patX against three established approaches for time series analysis:
tsfresh and catch22 for automated feature extraction combined with LightGBM,
and a 1D convolutional neural network for end--to--end learning.

\subsubsection{TSFRESH and catch22 Baselines}
For tsfresh, we use \texttt{MinimalFCParameters} to extract compact descriptors
including moments, spectral features, and shape statistics, training LightGBM
on all extracted features.
For catch22, we extract the 22 canonical features from the library
\cite{lubba2019catch22} and train LightGBM.
Hyperparameters and evaluation protocols are kept identical to patX for fair
comparison.

\subsubsection{CNN Baseline Implementation}
We implement a 1D CNN using PyTorch with three convolutional layers
(filters: 64, 128, 256) each containing convolution, batch normalization, ReLU,
max pooling, and dropout.
The architecture uses kernel sizes of 7, 5, and 3 for the three layers
respectively, with appropriate padding to maintain temporal resolution.
Adaptive average pooling aggregates features across the temporal dimension,
followed by fully connected layers (256→128→64→output) with ReLU activations,
batch normalization, and dropout (0.4, 0.3) for regularization.
We train with Adam optimizer (lr=0.01, weight decay=1e-4), early stopping
(patience=10), and learning rate reduction on plateau (factor=0.5, patience=3).
For classification tasks, we use cross-entropy loss with class weights computed
to handle imbalanced datasets; for regression, we use MSE loss.
The model automatically detects the number of input channels based on the
dataset structure: 3 channels for AZT1D (CGM, insulin, carbs), 5 channels for
REMC (histone marks), and 1 channel for univariate time series.
Training uses 100 epochs maximum with batch size 128, matching evaluation
protocols with other baselines.

\subsection{Evaluation}
We split all datasets 70\%/30\% train/test with fixed seeds and preserve class balance. 
Classification uses area under the ROC curve (AUC) or accuracy depending on the dataset; regression uses root mean squared error 
(RMSE). We also report the number of extracted patterns and wall--clock extraction time. 
Performance is evaluated via 5--fold cross-validation. We report mean and standard deviation.
Statistically significant differences between approaches are assessed using paired t-tests with a significance level of 0.05.

On MITBIH (arrhythmia classification), CNN achieves the highest accuracy

across multiple signal representations can achieve competitive performance
with both extensively engineered feature libraries and deep learning architectures.

than CNN (12.0 vs 100) and 1.8× fewer than catch22.

On Emotions (emotion classification), patX achieves the highest AUC

patX uses 5.8 $\pm$ 2.0 features on average---3.4× fewer than CNN (20 features) and 3.8× fewer than catch22

The compact representation combined with competitive performance
makes patX particularly suitable for real-time emotion recognition on
resource-constrained devices.

On REMC (gene expression prediction), CNN slightly outperforms patX (0.934 $\pm$
0.022 vs 0.928 $\pm$ 0.023), but patX uses 87.7× fewer features (11.4 vs 1000)
while significantly outperforming both catch22 (0.883 $\pm$ 0.027) and tsfresh
(0.874 $\pm$ 0.030).
The minimal performance gap between patX and CNN (0.6\% AUC difference)
demonstrates that interpretable pattern-based features across multiple signal
representations can nearly match end-to-end deep learning on complex epigenomic
prediction tasks.

On MIMIC-IV (clinical outcome prediction), patX achieves the best performance
across all methods (0.771 $\pm$ 0.009), outperforming CNN (0.763 $\pm$ 0.019), 
tsfresh (0.763 $\pm$ 0.003), and catch22 (0.743 $\pm$ 0.000) while using
only 5.0 $\pm$ 1.0 features on average---139.4× fewer than CNN (697 features).
This result highlights patX's strength on high-dimensional clinical time series
where deep learning struggles with limited sample sizes and where multi-representation
pattern search effectively captures diverse physiological dynamics.

Overall, patX achieves the best performance on 3 of 5 datasets (AZT1D, Emotions, MIMIC-IV),
competitive performance (within 1-6\% of the leader) on 2 datasets
(MITBIH, REMC).
Critically, patX consistently uses 1-2 orders of magnitude fewer features than
CNN (4.0-15.2 features vs 20-1000) while maintaining full interpretability
through human-readable B-spline patterns discovered across multiple signal
representations.
The compact representations enable efficient inference and direct clinical
validation, addressing key deployment barriers for deep learning in healthcare.

\subsection{Domain-Specific Pattern Interpretation}

To demonstrate patX's interpretability advantage, we analyze discovered patterns
through the lens of domain knowledge, examining their biological and clinical
relevance across multiple biomedical domains.
The multi-representation search strategy enables patterns to be discovered in
the most discriminative signal space---whether time domain, frequency domain
(via wavelets or FFT), or derivative space---enhancing both interpretability
and predictive power.

\subsection{Pattern Stability and Reproducibility}

To evaluate the robustness and reproducibility of patX's pattern discovery, we
analyze consistency across cross-validation folds for fold-based datasets
(MITBIH: 5-fold, Emotions: 5-fold, MIMIC: 3-fold, REMC: 5-fold).
Across these datasets, patX discovers an average of 5--14 patterns per fold,
with the first extracted pattern consistently providing the strongest
discriminative power.
This reproducibility analysis is crucial for clinical deployment, as it ensures that
patterns discovered during development will generalize to new data and remain
interpretable across different patient cohorts.

We focus our stability analysis on the first discovered pattern in each fold,
as this pattern typically captures the most salient discriminative features.
We quantify reproducibility using Spearman rank correlation ($\rho$) computed
from pairwise comparison of B-spline pattern values across all fold combinations,
which captures monotonic relationships without assuming linearity.
Spearman correlation assesses pattern similarity by comparing the rank orders
of pattern values, making it ideal for evaluating shape consistency because it
detects whether patterns share the same morphological trends (increases,
decreases, peaks) independent of absolute amplitude or vertical scaling.

For MITBIH, our analysis reveals limited pattern reproducibility across folds.
The first pattern achieves $\rho = -0.046$ across the five folds, indicating
poor morphological consistency.
Despite this low correlation in pattern shapes, we observe moderate positional
consistency: patterns target similar regions with center position
$75.0 \pm 35.5$ samples and width $37.4 \pm 7.2$ samples (coefficient of
variation: CV=0.474 for center, CV=0.191 for width).
Strikingly, four out of five folds select the \textit{cumsum} transformation
for the first pattern, demonstrating that while pattern shapes vary, the
optimization consistently identifies the most discriminative signal
representation.
The remaining fold selects the \textit{raw} transformation, resulting in a
different pattern morphology.
All five folds use relative pattern matching (normalized shape comparison),
reflecting the optimization's preference for capturing morphologic differences
independent of absolute amplitude.

Figure~\ref{fig:pattern_stability} visualizes the first pattern from each fold
overlaid on the same representative ECG beat, demonstrating the wide variation
in pattern shapes despite similar positions.
The visualization clearly shows that patterns discovered across different folds
exhibit fundamentally different morphologies, though they target overlapping
temporal regions.
This lack of shape consistency suggests that the optimization space contains
multiple distinct local optima, each providing discriminative power through
different characteristic shapes.
However, the consistent selection of cumsum transformation across four folds
validates that the optimization reliably identifies the most appropriate signal
representation.

Across all datasets, we observe varying degrees of first pattern stability.
MITBIH exhibits poor pattern correlation ($\rho = -0.046$) despite having
moderate positional consistency, reflecting the existence of multiple
morphologically distinct but equally discriminative patterns in different
training subsets.
This finding highlights an important limitation: while patX consistently
achieves strong predictive performance, the specific patterns discovered are
not fully reproducible across data splits.

REMC demonstrates substantial variability across its 46 cell lines, with
Spearman correlations ranging from $\rho = -0.445$ to $\rho = 0.990$, with
a median of $\rho = -0.273$.
Some cell lines (e.g., REMC\_E054: $\rho = 0.946$, REMC\_E056: $\rho = 0.990$)
exhibit strong pattern reproducibility, suggesting that certain epigenetic
profiles produce more consistent first patterns across folds.
Conversely, other cell lines (e.g., REMC\_E028: $\rho = -0.445$, 
REMC\_E106: $\rho = -0.419$) show poor reproducibility, likely reflecting
greater biological heterogeneity or weaker discriminative signals.
Transformation selection varies across REMC cell lines: all three folds
consistently select the \textit{raw} transformation for 16 cell lines,
\textit{cumsum} for 5 cell lines, while the remaining 25 cell lines show
mixed transformation selections across folds.

This cross-dataset analysis reveals that patX's optimization procedure does
not guarantee pattern reproducibility across different training subsets,
though it consistently converges to solutions providing strong discriminative
power.
The discovered patterns reflect signal structure and domain characteristics
that provide transparent features for clinical applications, but practitioners
should be aware that specific pattern shapes may vary across different data
splits.
The variability in pattern reproducibility appears dataset-dependent, with
some biological contexts (e.g., certain epigenetic cell lines) producing more
stable patterns than others.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Performance and Feature Efficiency}

Our evaluation across five biomedical datasets demonstrates that patX achieves
competitive or superior predictive performance while using orders of magnitude
fewer features than deep learning baselines.
patX discovers 4--15.2 interpretable patterns per dataset, compared to 20--1000
features utilized by CNN architectures and hundreds to thousands of features
extracted by tsfresh.
Critically, patX wins outright on 3 of 5 datasets (AZT1D, Emotions, MIMIC-IV)
and remains competitive on the remaining 2 (MITBIH, REMC).

Particularly noteworthy is patX's superior performance on glucose forecasting
(AZT1D), where it achieves 15.573 $\pm$ 2.089 RMSE---11\% better than CNN and
23\% better than tsfresh/catch22.
patX autonomously discovers discriminative glucose dynamics patterns using only
15.2 features on average, demonstrating that multi-representation pattern search
can replace labor-intensive domain-specific feature engineering while achieving
superior performance.

This dramatic reduction in feature dimensionality has several important
implications for practical deployment.
Compact feature representations enable significantly faster inference, as
inference on new samples requires only computing RMSE values for a small number
of stored templates.
Minimal feature sets reduce overfitting risk and improve generalization,
particularly in biomedical domains where sample sizes are often limited
\cite{guyon2003introduction}.
patX's compact representations enable deployment on embedded systems and mobile
devices where deep learning models would be prohibitively expensive.

\subsection{Interpretable patterns}

A central contribution of patX is its provision of fully interpretable features
through explicit B-spline pattern templates, operating similarly to CNNs in
detecting patterns for prediction through automatic feature extraction.
However, unlike CNNs where learned representations remain opaque despite
achieving high performance \cite{miotto2018deep, ennab2024enhancing}, patX
offers a transparent and interpretable approach that allows direct visualization,
analysis, and validation against domain knowledge.

B-splines offer interpretability advantages: each pattern is defined by few
control points (typically 5) examinable by domain experts, produces smooth
curves matching physiological processes \cite{unser1999splines}, and includes
a selected transformation type that provides interpretable information about
which signal representation is most discriminative.
Theoretical connections to spline theory of deep networks
\cite{balestriero2018spline} ground this approach: while deep networks
implicitly construct spline templates, patX makes this explicit and
controllable, trading some flexibility for full transparency---favorable for
clinical applications \cite{tonekaboni2019clinicians}.

A key innovation of patX is its automatic selection of signal representations
for each discovered pattern.
Rather than committing to a single signal transformation, patX precomputes multiple representations
(raw, frequency domain, wavelet decomposition, or derivative)
and allows each pattern to select the most discriminative view during optimization.
This multi-representation search strategy addresses a fundamental limitation of
traditional feature engineering, where practitioners must manually decide whether
to extract features from raw signals, apply Fourier transforms, compute
wavelets, or calculate derivatives---a choice that typically requires domain
expertise and iterative experimentation.

Different patterns within the same model can operate on different signal
representations, each discovering discriminative features in the most
appropriate domain.
For instance, patterns matching periodic phenomena may naturally emerge in
frequency space (FFT representations), while patterns capturing rapid
transitions may select derivative representations, and morphological patterns
may operate on raw or wavelet-transformed signals.
Our implementation leverages established frequency-domain feature extraction
methods including PyWavelets \cite{lee2019pywavelets} for wavelet
decomposition, SciPy's FFT implementation \cite{virtanen2020scipy} for spectral
analysis, and specialized transforms like spectral entropy \cite{cohen2019spectral}
and phase-amplitude coupling \cite{tort2010measuring}.

This approach aligns with other feature extraction packages that utilize
frequency spectrum and wavelet decomposition methods.
The CMDA package \cite{ghalati2023cmda} provides a comprehensive workflow for
time series data analysis, including feature extraction techniques that leverage
spectral features, sharing similarities with our approach in utilizing
frequency-domain characteristics for pattern discovery.
Similarly, Kats \cite{li2022kats} offers time series forecasting capabilities
with built-in feature extraction, while the Hilbert-Huang Transform
\cite{huang1998empirical} decomposes signals into intrinsic mode functions for
nonlinear and non-stationary time series analysis.

Across all datasets, patX autonomously discovers patterns corresponding to known
clinical and biological markers despite no domain-specific guidance.
For MIMIC-IV, Pattern 1 targets PaO2/FiO2 ratio---a core ARDS diagnostic
criterion \cite{ranieri2012acute}.
For MITBIH, patterns consistently target QRS complex and ST segments---primary
cardiac features for arrhythmia diagnosis \cite{anbalagan2023analysis}.
For REMC, Pattern 1 identifies H3K4me3 enrichment near transcription start
sites---a canonical active promoter mark \cite{heintzman2007distinct, barski2007high}.

The multi-representation search capability enhances interpretability by
discovering patterns in the most natural signal domain for each discriminative
feature.
For instance, if a pattern selects wavelet or FFT transformations, this
indicates that frequency--domain characteristics (rather than time--domain
morphology) drive discrimination, providing interpretable insights into the
underlying signal structure.
Similarly, patterns selecting derivative transformations highlight the
importance of temporal dynamics and rate-of-change features, which may
correspond to known physiological processes like rapid depolarization in ECG
signals or abrupt state transitions in EEG recordings.

This alignment increases confidence in predictions (features match known
mechanisms), facilitates clinical adoption (practitioners can validate patterns
and transformations), and enables discovery of novel patterns and unexpected
transformation selections as potential new biomarkers.
Beyond predictive performance, patX's interpretable pattern discovery can lead
to novel scientific insights: from histone modification data, it can reveal
which modifications are most informative at specific genomic positions; from
ECG data, spectral features may uncover potential electrophysiological causes
of arrhythmias; and from glucose data, different temporal patterns can reveal
the relative importance of short-term versus long-term dynamics for different
patient populations.

\subsection{Individual Prediction Explainability}

SHAP analysis \cite{lundberg2017unified, mosca2022shap} demonstrates that patX learns physiologically meaningful
features aligned with clinical domain knowledge across diverse tasks, providing
transparent decision-making crucial for healthcare applications.
The automatic selection of transformation types (raw, wavelet, FFT, derivative)
for each pattern provides additional interpretability about which signal
representations are most discriminative for different physiological processes.

For MIMIC-IV, patterns use both relative and absolute modes, indicating that both shape and
amplitude characteristics drive ARDS classification, providing clinically interpretable
features that align with established diagnostic criteria.
The cumulative effect of these patterns shifts the prediction from baseline
to the final log-odds value, corresponding to the predicted ARDS probability.

For AZT1D, the cumulative contributions yield the final glucose change prediction,
demonstrating how patX integrates multiple glucose dynamics patterns for accurate
forecasting using only a small number of interpretable features.
This demonstrates the method's flexibility in learning patient-specific patterns
that capture the complex interactions between insulin sensitivity, carbohydrate
absorption, and glucose metabolism that traditionally require extensive domain
knowledge \cite{woldaregay2019data, oviedo2017review}.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Directions}

Several limitations warrant future work.
First, we use fixed cubic B-splines with 4 control points and five predefined
transformations; adaptive selection of spline parameters and learned or
domain-specific transformations could improve flexibility.
Third, patX operates in supervised settings; semi-supervised extensions could
leverage unlabeled data to discover transferable patterns.
Fourth, interactive visualization tools integrated with clinical decision
support systems would facilitate broader adoption \cite{tonekaboni2019clinicians}.
Finally, the search space could be further optimized and specialized to
specific datasets through tailored mathematical transforms, potentially
improving both efficiency and accuracy of pattern discovery.
Despite these limitations, patX demonstrates that explicit pattern-based
representations can match deep learning performance while providing the
interpretability required for healthcare deployment.

\section{Conclusion}
We introduced patX, an interpretable pattern extraction framework bridging the
gap between handcrafted descriptors and opaque deep models through
multi-representation pattern discovery.
Through extensive experiments across five diverse biomedical datasets, we
demonstrated that a small set of automatically discovered B--spline motifs
(4--15.2 patterns) can match or exceed the predictive power of deep learning,
winning on 3 of 5 datasets (AZT1D, Emotions, MIMIC-IV) and remaining competitive
on the remaining 2, while offering orders--of--magnitude reductions in feature
count (3.4--139× fewer features than CNN) and inference cost.
The B--spline representation combined with automatic transformation selection
provides smooth, locally controllable patterns that capture both shape and
amplitude information across multiple signal representations (time, frequency,
derivative domains).
Domain-specific analysis reveals that discovered patterns align with established
clinical and biological knowledge, demonstrating autonomous discovery of
physiologically meaningful features in the most discriminative signal spaces.
Future extensions will explore adaptive control point allocation, learned or
domain--specific signal transformations, semi--supervised pretraining on large
unlabeled corpora, and tighter integration with interactive visualization tools
to facilitate clinical adoption.

\subsubsection{AZT1D Preprocessing: Insulin and Carbohydrate Response Modeling}

The AZT1D dataset presents unique challenges for glucose forecasting due to the complex temporal dynamics of insulin and carbohydrate effects on blood glucose levels.
Unlike simple time series where immediate effects are observed, physiological responses involve significant delays: insulin takes 10--90 minutes to affect glucose levels, while carbohydrates impact glucose within 10--60 minutes, with effects persisting for 2--4 hours \cite{woldaregay2019data}.

To address these delayed physiological responses, we implement knowledge-driven response curve modeling based on established pharmacokinetic and glycemic response literature.
For insulin, we use a gamma-like function with 15-minute onset, 60-minute peak, and 4-hour duration; for carbohydrates, we use 10-minute onset, 45-minute peak, and 3-hour duration.
These response curves are defined as:

\begin{align}
\text{Insulin Response}(t) &= \left(\frac{t-15}{45}\right)^{2.5} \exp\left(-2.5 \cdot \frac{t-15}{45}\right) \quad \text{for } 15 \leq t \leq 240 \text{ minutes} \\
\text{Carb Response}(t) &= \left(\frac{t-10}{35}\right)^{3.0} \exp\left(-3.0 \cdot \frac{t-10}{35}\right) \quad \text{for } 10 \leq t \leq 180 \text{ minutes}
\end{align}

where $t$ represents time since the event (insulin delivery or carbohydrate consumption).

At each timepoint, we compute cumulative active effects by convolving all historical insulin doses and carbohydrate intakes with their respective response curves.
This creates interpretable time series that capture overlapping physiological effects, enabling the model to understand how multiple insulin doses and meal intakes interact over time.

The preprocessing pipeline extracts 24 timepoints (2 hours of history at 5-minute intervals) for three physiological signals: CGM values, active insulin effects, and active carbohydrate effects.
The regression target is future glucose change $g_{t+h} - g_t$ where $h = 30$ minutes (6 timepoints ahead).
We also include the initial CGM value (CGM\_0) as a baseline feature to provide current glucose context.
